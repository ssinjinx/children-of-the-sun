The storm gathered slowly at first, then with terrifying speed. Set's forces consolidated their hold on the northern districts, establishing checkpoints and curfews, rooting out those who opposed his rule. The Children of Darkness, deprived of their leadership by Sekhet's capture, splintered into cells—each operating independently, each dangerous in its own way.

Ra and Issah prepared as best they could. The safe house became a fortress, its modest exterior hiding defenses that Kiya had designed over years of paranoia. Traps, escape routes, hidden supplies—they had planned for every contingency except the one that mattered most.

Aten was growing faster than any normal child. At three weeks old, she had the alertness of a three-month-old, the strength of a six-month-old. She recognized her parents, responded to her name, reached for objects with coordination that shouldn't have been possible.

"The hybrid development," Thoth observed, his instruments recording every milestone. "It's accelerating. She may reach physical maturity in years rather than decades."

"Is that dangerous?" Issah asked, holding her daughter close.

"It's unprecedented. Which means we don't know." Thoth's honesty was as unsettling as any lie could have been. "Her body is rewriting the rules as it grows. We can only watch and adapt."

The signal from the stars grew stronger with each passing day, Thoth's instruments detecting patterns that suggested intelligence, purpose, urgency. It was no longer a passive beacon—it was reaching out, trying to establish contact, trying to communicate.

"It's asking for her," Thoth reported, his voice hushed with awe. "The signal is specifically requesting a biological response from Aten. It's as if... as if it knows her."

"Can we block it?" Ra demanded.

"We can dampen it, delay it. But we can't stop it entirely. The connection is... fundamental. At the quantum level. It's as if she was designed to receive it."

"She wasn't designed," Issah said fiercely. "She was born. From love. Not from some cosmic plan."

"Perhaps love is the plan," Thoth suggested, and for once, his wisdom sounded like poetry.

The days blurred together as they prepared for the inevitable confrontation. Ra trained with Kiya, learning to fight in his weakened state, compensating for lost strength with increased cunning. Issah worked with Meritamen, coordinating the resistance, building networks of communication and support.

And Aten grew, and watched, and learned.

She was two months old when she first spoke—a single word, "Light," that she whispered while staring at the sun through the window. She was three months old when she first walked, toddling across the room to collapse laughing in Ra's arms.

"She's not human," Kiya observed, watching the child play.

"She's not Shetu either," Issah replied. "She's something new. Something the world has never seen."

"And the world is afraid of her."

"The world is afraid of everything it doesn't understand."

The attack came on the night of the dark moon, when the sky was clouded and the stars hidden. Set's forces struck from the east, the Children of Darkness from the west, converging on the safe house with coordinated precision that suggested someone had betrayed their location.

"Thoth," Ra cursed, even as he knew it wasn't true. The god of wisdom had been with them all day, his instruments humming as he monitored the signal's increasing intensity.

"Not Thoth," Kiya said, appearing from the shadows with her weapons drawn. "Sekhet. She escaped three hours ago. We just received word."

"She led them here."

"She led them here."

The first explosions rocked the house, dust falling from the ceiling, Aten waking with a cry that seemed to echo with more than infant fear.

"Get her out," Ra commanded, even as he moved to the window to assess the threat. "The tunnel in the cellar. Kiya knows the way."

"I'm not leaving you," Issah said.

"You're not leaving her." He turned to face them, his wife and child, the two reasons he had surrendered godhood, the two reasons he would fight until his last breath. "Go. I'll hold them as long as I can."

"Ra—"

"Go!"

The word carried the last echo of his divine authority, the command of a god who had once ruled the world. Issah hesitated, then nodded, gathering Aten and following Kiya toward the cellar.

Ra turned to face the door, drawing the sword he had carried since his mortal transformation. It was not a divine weapon, not a tool of cosmic power. It was steel and bronze, forged by human hands, as mortal as he had become.

But in his grip, it would be enough.

The door splintered, and the storm finally broke.
