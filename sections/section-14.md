The revelation came not from Kiya's investigation but from a mistake—a moment of carelessness that exposed everything. It was Meritamen who found it, the Council Speaker who had supported them from the beginning, her ancient eyes still sharp enough to catch what others missed.

She came to their house at midnight, wrapped in darkness and urgency, her face pale in the lamplight.

"It's Sekhet," she said without preamble. "Your healer. She's been feeding information to the Children of Darkness since the beginning."

The words hit like a physical blow. Ra felt the room tilt, his recently recovered strength suddenly insufficient.

"Impossible," Issah said, her hand moving to her belly. "She's been treating me, treating the child. She's saved both our lives."

"And she's been reporting every detail to the enemy." Meritamen produced a bundle of documents, papyrus sheets covered in Sekhet's distinctive hand. "I found these in her chambers. Detailed notes on your condition, your location, your vulnerabilities. And this—"

She held up a final sheet, smaller than the others, covered in code. "A message she was preparing to send. It names the night when they plan to strike. Three days from now, when the moon is dark."

Ra took the documents with trembling hands, reading the betrayal in Sekhet's clinical descriptions: Issah's strength failing, the child's growth irregular, Ra's weakness after each blood donation. Every intimate detail of their struggle recorded and reported to those who would destroy them.

"Why?" he whispered.

"Because she believes in the old ways," Meritamen said. "Because she sees your child as an abomination that must be eliminated. Because she thinks she's saving the Shetu from corruption."

"She's been in our home," Issah said, her voice hollow. "She's touched me, examined me, pretended to care. All while she was planning our deaths."

"She's not the only one." Meritamen's face was grave. "Khufu, Anhert—they're part of it, yes. But there are others. High-ranking members of the council, the military, the temples. The Children of Darkness have been patient, building their network for decades. Your child simply gave them a focus, a reason to accelerate their plans."

"And Horus?" Ra asked. "He claimed to withdraw his support."

"He did. But the Children of Darkness don't need his money anymore. They've found other sources, other patrons. The conspiracy has grown beyond any single benefactor."

Ra moved to the window, staring out at the darkness. Three days. In three days, while the moon hid its face, his enemies would come for his family. And the woman he had trusted with their lives would lead them to the door.

"We need to move," he said finally. "Tonight. Get Issah to safety before Sekhet realizes we know."

"And then?"

"And then we end this." Ra turned back to face them, and Issah saw something new in his eyes—not the desperate fear of recent weeks, but the cold determination of a god who had once ruled the world. "We use their own plan against them. We let them think they're winning, right up until the moment we destroy them."

"How?"

"Sekhet doesn't know we've discovered her. She thinks she still has the advantage." Ra's smile was sharp as broken glass. "So we give her what she wants. We let her lead the Children of Darkness into a trap of our own making."

The plan came together quickly, fueled by betrayal and the desperate need to protect what remained. Meritamen used her position to spread false information, carefully crafted to reach Sekhet's ears. Kiya prepared their allies, positioning them throughout the city. And Ra made one final visit to Set, his brother's network of informants proving unexpectedly useful.

"You're gambling everything," Set observed, watching Ra outline the strategy.

"I've gambled everything before."

"And lost, more often than not."

Ra met his brother's eyes. "Not this time. This time, I have something worth winning for."

Set was silent for a long moment, then nodded slowly. "The ambush point—my old temple, where the Children of Darkness have been meeting. It's defensible, but not impregnable. If they realize it's a trap..."

"They won't. Sekhet's arrogance is her weakness. She believes she's too clever to be caught, too careful to be exposed. She'll lead them right where we want them."

"And if she doesn't?"

"Then we improvise." Ra placed a hand on Set's shoulder, feeling the familiar tension of muscles that had once tried to kill him. "Thank you. For helping, despite everything."

Set's expression flickered—surprise, perhaps, or something deeper. "I'm not helping you, brother. I'm helping the future. Whatever that child becomes, it deserves a chance to exist."

"Even if it's an abomination?"

"Especially then." Set smiled, and for the first time, it reached his eyes. "The Shetu have grown stagnant, Ra. We've been dying slowly for millennia, clinging to traditions that no longer serve us. If your child represents change, then perhaps change is what we need."

They parted as the sun rose, Set disappearing into the desert, Ra returning to the city and the final preparations. Issah met him at the door of their house, her bag already packed, her face set with determination.

"I'm ready," she said.

"Are you sure? Once we start this, there's no going back."

"There never was." She took his hand, pressing it against her belly where their child moved with increasing strength. "We do this together, or not at all."

"Together," he agreed.

They left the house as the morning market began to stir, blending into the crowd like any other couple seeking the day's provisions. Behind them, in the shadows, Kiya watched their departure and sent the signal that would begin the endgame.

Sekhet found the house empty when she arrived for her scheduled visit, her medical bag in hand, her face composed in its usual mask of professional concern. She didn't realize the trap had been sprung until Kiya's blade was at her throat, until Meritamen's guards surrounded her, until she saw the documents spread across the table that proved her guilt beyond any doubt.

"You can't stop it," she spat, her composure finally cracking. "The wheels are already in motion. In three days, your abomination will be dead, and the old order restored."

"In three days," Kiya replied, binding her wrists with practiced efficiency, "your organization will be destroyed, your leaders exposed, and your cause defeated. You underestimated them, Sekhet. You underestimated what love can build."

"Love?" The healer laughed, harsh and bitter. "Love is weakness. Love is the poison that destroyed the Shetu, that made them soft and foolish and mortal."

"Love is strength," Ra said, stepping from the shadows where he had watched her capture. "Love is what makes mortals fight gods, what makes parents protect children, what makes the impossible possible. You never understood that, Sekhet. That's why you'll lose."

She stared at him, hatred burning in her ancient eyes. "Then kill me. End it now."

"No." Ra's voice was cold. "You'll live to see your failure. You'll watch as everything you fought for crumbles, as the future you feared becomes the world you must live in. That's your punishment—not death, but irrelevance."

They took her away, struggling and cursing, to a cell where she would wait until the trap was sprung. Ra watched her go, feeling no satisfaction in the victory, only weariness and the hollow ache of betrayed trust.

"One down," Kiya said. "Many more to go."

"Yes." Ra turned to Issah, who waited in the doorway with the sun behind her, glowing like the goddess she had become in his eyes. "But the hardest part is over. We've identified the enemy. Now we just have to defeat them."

"Just," Issah repeated, smiling despite everything. "You make it sound so simple."

"Nothing worth having is simple." He took her hand, and together they stepped into the morning, into the war that would decide their child's future, into the uncertain promise of tomorrow.

The traitor was revealed. The trap was set. And in three days, when the moon hid its face, the fate of their world would be decided.
