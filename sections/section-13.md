The meeting took place in neutral territory—a merchant's villa on the outskirts of Memphis, owned by a man who owed favors to both sides and could be trusted to keep secrets. Ra arrived with Kiya and two guards, his body still weak but his mind sharp with determination.

Horus was already there, seated in the villa's central courtyard with the sun at his back, his golden armor catching the light like a second sun. He looked every inch the god he had become—perfect, powerful, untouchable.

"You asked for this meeting," Horus said, not rising. "Speak your piece, former lord."

Ra took the seat across from him, ignoring the deliberate insult. "I know about the money, Horus. I know you're funding the Children of Darkness."

The young god's expression didn't change, but Ra saw the slight tightening around his eyes. "You have proof?"

"Enough to make accusations that would damage you, even if they couldn't destroy you." Ra leaned forward, letting his weakness show, using it as a weapon. "But I'm not here to accuse. I'm here to understand."

"Understand what?"

"Why." Ra spread his hands on the table between them. "You have everything—power, worship, the sun itself. Why would you support those who want to kill my child?"

Horus was silent for a long moment, his perfect face unreadable. Then, surprisingly, he laughed—a bitter sound that held no humor. "You really don't see it, do you? You, who gave up everything for love, who threw away eight millennia of power to be with a human. You have no idea what it's like to be me."

"Enlighten me."

"I was born to be your heir." Horus stood, pacing to the edge of the courtyard and staring out at the city below. "From my first breath, I was trained to take your place, to wear the mantle you would eventually set down. I learned the prayers, the rituals, the cosmic responsibilities. I prepared for a role that would last until the end of time."

"And then I gave it away."

"And then you gave it away." Horus turned back, and Ra saw the pain in his eyes, the resentment that had festered for years. "Not to me, not to your chosen successor, but to no one. You simply... stopped. And I was left with a throne that had no precedent, a power that had no guide."

"So you turned to the Children of Darkness?"

"I turned to order!" Horus's voice rose, carrying the weight of divine authority. "The revolution you supported destroyed everything stable, everything certain. The Shetu are dying, Ra. Our bloodlines thin, our worship fades, our power leaks away like water from a cracked jar. The Children of Darkness offer a solution—a return to the old ways, to the separation that kept us strong."

"By killing my child?"

"By preventing an abomination that would destroy the last barriers between mortal and divine!" Horus moved closer, his intensity palpable. "Don't you understand? Your child represents the end of everything. If humans and Shetu can breed, if divine blood can be diluted and mixed, then we become nothing more than a different kind of mortal. We lose our essence, our identity, our reason for existing."

Ra stood to meet him, ignoring the dizziness that threatened to overwhelm him. "And if the old ways are killing you? If the separation you cherish is actually the poison that's destroying the Shetu?"

"Then we die with dignity!" Horus shouted. "Better to end as we were than to become something else, something lesser, something grotesque!"

The silence that followed was heavy with unspoken threats. Kiya's hand moved to her weapon, and Ra saw Horus's guards tense in response.

"There is another way," Ra said quietly. "A middle path. The child doesn't have to mean the end of the Shetu. It could mean a new beginning."

"Words." Horus spat. "Pretty words from a man who threw away his birthright."

"From a man who learned that birthright isn't everything." Ra held Horus's gaze, letting the young god see the truth in his eyes. "I was you once, Horus. I believed that power was everything, that godhood was the only thing that mattered. And I was wrong. The love I have with Issah, the child we're creating together—that's worth more than all the suns in the sky."

"Then you're a fool."

"Perhaps. But I'm a fool with nothing left to lose." Ra stepped closer, close enough to smell the divine essence that still clung to Horus like perfume. "If you continue to support the Children of Darkness, if you continue to threaten my family, I will use every resource I have left to destroy you. I may be mortal now, but I remember things, Horus. Secrets that could shatter your worship, undermine your authority, turn your own priests against you."

"You would start a war?"

"I would end one." Ra's voice was steel. "By any means necessary."

They stood there, former god and current deity, the sun's past and present facing each other across an unbridgeable gap. Horus's hands were clenched at his sides, his divine power barely contained, and Ra knew that a single word could end him where he stood.

But he didn't flinch. He had faced death before, had chosen mortality over immortality. The threat of destruction held no terror for him anymore.

"You love her that much," Horus said finally. It wasn't a question.

"I love them that much. Issah, the child, the future we're trying to build. Yes."

Horus turned away, staring at the sun he commanded. "I could kill you now. No one would know."

"They would know. Kiya knows. My guards know. And Issah would never stop until she found the truth."

"Your human queen." Horus's voice was distant. "She has changed you."

"She has saved me."

Another long silence. Then Horus turned back, and something had shifted in his expression—not acceptance, not friendship, but perhaps a grudging respect.

"I will withdraw my support from the Children of Darkness," he said. "Not because I agree with you, but because you're right about one thing—war would destroy us all."

"And the child?"

"I won't protect it. But I won't actively seek its destruction either." Horus's eyes met Ra's. "Consider that my gift to you, former lord. A neutrality you haven't earned."

"It's enough."

"It will have to be." Horus moved toward the exit, his guards falling into step behind him. At the doorway, he paused. "For what it's worth... I hope you're right. I hope your child represents a future worth having. Because if you're wrong, if this path leads only to destruction, then your sacrifice will have been for nothing."

"Then we'll have to make sure I'm right."

Horus nodded once, sharply, and was gone.

Ra sank back into his chair, his legs trembling with the aftermath of adrenaline. Kiya was at his side instantly, offering water, support, concern.

"You took a risk," she said. "He could have killed you."

"He could have." Ra drank deeply, feeling his heart rate slowly return to normal. "But he didn't. And now we have a chance—a real chance—to stop the Children of Darkness without open war."

"Neutrality isn't victory."

"No. But it's not defeat either." Ra stood, leaning on Kiya's arm for support. "We take what we can get, and we keep fighting for the rest."

They left the villa as the sun began to set, painting the sky in shades of gold and crimson. Ra looked up at the sun he had once commanded, now wielded by a younger god who had chosen a different path.

They weren't enemies anymore. Not quite allies, either. But somewhere in the middle, there was space to build something new.

And that, Ra thought, was where the future always began.
