The death of Ra was not an ending but a transformation, and like all transformations, it took time to understand. Issah spent the first year learning to feel his presence in the bridge, in the light, in their daughter's eyes. She learned to speak to him, not expecting answers but finding comfort in the speaking itself.

"I miss you," she would say, alone in their small house, Aten sleeping in the next room. "I miss your hands, your voice, your warmth beside me at night."

And sometimes, if she was very still, if she opened her heart completely, she would feel him—like sunlight on her skin, like warmth in winter, like love made tangible.

"I know," the feeling would say. "I miss you too. But I'm here. I'm always here."

The second year brought changes. Aten grew into a child who was wise beyond her years, who could speak with the ancestors and counsel the living, who walked between worlds as easily as others walked between rooms. She became the heart of the new civilization, the symbol of what was possible when love transcended boundaries.

"Papa says he's proud of you," Aten told her mother one evening, as they watched the sun set over the Nile. "He says you're building something beautiful. Something that will last."

"I couldn't do it without you," Issah replied. "Without both of you."

"You could," Aten said seriously. "But you don't have to. That's what family means."

The third year brought visitors from the ancestral world—Shetu who had returned and found what they sought, who came back to share knowledge, to build connections, to thank the child who had made it possible. They brought word of Set, who had changed in ways none of them had expected.

"He's become a teacher," one visitor reported. "He studies the old ways, the true history of our people. He says he spent so long trying to be powerful that he forgot how to be wise."

Issah smiled at that. Ra would have appreciated the irony.

The years continued, each bringing new challenges, new joys, new reasons to remember what had been sacrificed and what had been gained. The bridge remained open, the connection between worlds growing stronger with each passing day. Civilizations rose on both sides, learning from each other, growing together.

And through it all, Ra was there—not as a god, not as a ruler, but as love itself. The love that had made the bridge possible, that had given everything so that the future might exist.

On the fifth anniversary of his sacrifice, Issah took Aten to the place where it had happened—the courtyard of the ruined house, now transformed into a garden of light. Flowers that glowed with soft radiance grew there, fed by the energy of the bridge, tended by those who remembered.

"He was here," Aten said, her voice hushed with wonder. "I can feel him so strongly. He's... he's happy, Mama. Truly happy."

"I know." Issah knelt beside her daughter, placing her hand on the warm earth. "He found what he was looking for. Not power. Not immortality. But connection. Love. Purpose."

"He found you," Aten said. "And through you, he found everything else."

They sat together in the garden of light, mother and daughter, the living and the bridge, feeling the presence of the one who had made it all possible. And as the sun set and the stars emerged, Issah felt something she hadn't expected—peace.

Not the peace of forgetting. She would never forget. Not the peace of healing, for some wounds never fully close. But the peace of understanding, of acceptance, of knowing that love was stronger than death, stronger than time, stronger than any sacrifice.

Ra had died. But he had also become immortal in a way that no godhood could have provided. He was part of everything now, woven into the fabric of existence itself, his love the foundation for a new world.

"Goodbye, my love," she whispered, though it wasn't goodbye. It was never goodbye. "Until we meet again."

And in the light that surrounded them, in the bridge that connected everything, she felt his answer—warm, eternal, full of love.

"I'm here," he said. "I'm always here."
