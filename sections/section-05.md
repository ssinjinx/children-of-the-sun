The council chamber still bore the scars of revolution. Ra traced his fingers along a deep gouge in the limestone wall where a blade had struck during the uprising, leaving a pale scar in the darker stone. Two years ago, he would have been able to repair such damage with a mere thought, his divine will reshaping matter like clay. Now, as a mortal, he could only feel the rough edges beneath his fingertips, another reminder of all he had surrendered.

The emergency session would begin soon. Already, the curved rows of benches were filling with representatives from both human and Shetu factions, their whispered conversations creating a low hum that vibrated in Ra's chest. He had forgotten, in his centuries of godhood, how loud mortals could be when gathered in numbers—the shuffling of sandals, the rustle of linen, the sharp intake of breath before speech.

Issah sat at the center of the dais, her face a careful mask of composure despite the dark circles under her eyes. She hadn't slept since they'd found Amenra's body, and Ra could see the exhaustion in the slight tremor of her hands as she arranged her notes. When she caught his gaze, she offered a small smile—reassurance for him, or perhaps for herself—but he could see the fear beneath it, coiled tight as a serpent.

He took his place on the bench reserved for advisors, noting how Issah unconsciously placed a hand over her abdomen as she sat. The gesture went unnoticed by most of the chamber, but Ra felt a spike of protective fear. Their child, their impossible hybrid child, was still their most closely guarded secret. If the council knew of Issah's pregnancy, it would only add fuel to the already volatile political situation.

"They're afraid," Issah murmured, leaning toward him as the chamber continued to fill. Her voice was barely audible above the ambient noise, but he heard every word. "Look at how they cluster—humans with humans, Shetu with Shetu. Amenra's death has undone months of progress in a single night."

Before Ra could respond, Kiya slipped into the chamber through a side entrance, her leather investigator's gear looking out of place among the ceremonial robes and formal linens. She caught Ra's eye across the room and gave an almost imperceptible shake of her head—no new leads, then. The killer remained as elusive as smoke, and the council would demand answers he couldn't provide.

The chamber fell silent as Meritamen, the Council Speaker, rose to her feet. She was one of the few pure-blooded Shetu who had supported the revolution from its earliest days, her silver-white hair and severe robes marking her as one of the old guard who had chosen to embrace change rather than fight it. Ra had known her for three centuries, had watched her grow from an idealistic youth to a pragmatic leader. Now, as she surveyed the chamber with eyes that missed nothing, he wondered how much of her idealism remained.

"We are gathered," Meritamen began, her voice carrying to every corner of the high-ceilinged hall without apparent effort, "to address the murder of Amenra, First Advisor to the Transitional Government and architect of our new accord between humans and Shetu. This act of violence strikes at the very heart of everything we have worked to build."

Ra watched the faces around him, searching for any hint of guilt, any flicker of satisfaction at Amenra's death. As a god, he had been able to sense the emotional states of mortals, to taste the flavors of their intentions like wine on his tongue. Now he was left with only his eyes and his instincts, and the latter were screaming that someone in this very room had betrayed them.

"The method of killing," Meritamen continued, "suggests intimate knowledge of Shetu anatomy. The precision of the wound, the choice of weapon, the very location within our most secure chambers—all point to a level of access and education that cannot be easily explained."

A murmur of unease rippled through the assembly. Ra saw several council members shift uncomfortably, while others leaned toward their neighbors in urgent whispers. The seeds of suspicion had been planted; now they would watch them grow into full-blown paranoia.

"The Architect has claimed responsibility," called out Sekhet, one of the Shetu hardliners who had opposed the revolution from the beginning. He was ancient even by Shetu standards, his skin like weathered parchment stretched over prominent bones. "Why do we pretend this requires investigation? The radicals have grown bold. They must be crushed before they strike again."

"And how would you propose to crush them?" asked Khenmu, Issah's human advisor. His voice was measured, but Ra could hear the anger beneath it. "Round up every dissident in Memphis? Create martyrs for their cause? We've seen where that path leads."

"It leads to order," Sekhet snapped. "Something this council seems to have forgotten in its rush to embrace chaos."

"Enough." Issah's voice cut through the rising tension like a blade. She rose from her seat, and Ra saw her deliberately straighten her posture, projecting a confidence he knew she didn't feel. "We will not descend into factional bickering while Amenra's killer walks free. Kiya, report."

The investigator stepped forward, her leather sandals making no sound on the stone floor. She carried no notes, Ra noticed—everything was committed to memory, or perhaps to that wax tablet she kept tucked in her belt.

"The evidence suggests the killer had inside knowledge," Kiya stated, her sharp gaze moving from face to face around the chamber. "They knew Amenra's schedule, knew the side entrance would be unguarded, knew precisely where to strike to ensure a slow death. This was not the work of a random radical. This was personal."

"Personal?" The voice came from the back of the chamber, and Ra turned to see Horus rising from his seat. The young god who had inherited the sun's mantle was beautiful in the way of all Shetu—golden-skinned, perfect-featured, his eyes the color of desert sky. But there was something cold in those eyes now, something calculating. "Are you suggesting one of us is responsible, investigator?"

"I am suggesting we consider all possibilities," Kiya replied evenly, not intimidated by the divine presence before her. "Including the possibility that someone in this chamber, or connected to this chamber, provided aid to the killer."

The silence that followed was profound. Ra felt his heart pounding in his chest—a mortal sensation he still wasn't accustomed to—and forced himself to breathe slowly. He remembered the note they had found: 'The falcon watches.' Horus's symbol was the falcon, his very name associated with the sacred bird. But to accuse a god without proof...

"This is an outrage," Horus said, his voice dropping to a dangerous whisper. "You invite us here to discuss the murder of our colleague, and instead you accuse us of conspiracy? Perhaps the investigator should investigate herself."

"No one has accused anyone," Issah interjected, her tone firm but diplomatic. "We are simply acknowledging facts. The killer had help. Someone in a position of trust betrayed that trust. Our task is to discover who, and why, before more blood is spilled."

"Before more Shetu blood is spilled, you mean." This from a human delegate named Bes, a former slave who had risen to prominence during the revolution. His face was scarred from old whippings, his eyes hard with resentment. "How many humans died during the uprising? How many were sacrificed to Shetu hunger before Issah's reforms? One dead advisor seems a small price for justice."

"Justice?" Sekhet's voice rose to a shout. "This was murder, plain and simple! And you sit here and speak of justice as if Amenra were nothing?"

The chamber erupted into chaos. Accusations flew like arrows—humans blaming Shetu for centuries of oppression, Shetu accusing humans of ingratitude and violence. Ra watched as years of careful bridge-building crumbled in moments, old wounds torn open by fresh blood.

"Silence!" Issah's voice cut through the tumult, and something in her tone—the authority of someone who had led armies, who had faced death and returned—brought the room to stillness. "We are not here to settle old scores. We are here to find a killer. If we cannot do that, if we let fear and suspicion destroy what we've built, then Amenra died for nothing."

She looked around the chamber, meeting each gaze in turn. Ra felt a surge of pride mixed with fear. She was magnificent in her fury, a true leader. But she was also vulnerable, carrying their child, and the enemies arrayed against them were closing in.

"I propose a compromise," said a new voice, smooth as oil on water. The crowd parted to reveal Set, Ra's brother, entering through the main doors as if he had been invited rather than intruding. He was dressed in crimson and gold, his dark hair bound with copper wire, his smile sharp enough to draw blood. "A way to satisfy both those who seek justice and those who fear persecution."

Ra felt his blood run cold. Set had not been seen in Memphis since the revolution, had retreated to his desert stronghold to lick his wounds and plot his return. For him to appear now, at this moment...

"Set," Meritamen said, her voice carefully neutral. "You were not summoned to this council."

"No," Set agreed, moving to the center of the floor with the casual confidence of a predator among prey. "But I have information that this council needs. Information about the Architect, and about who might be helping them from within."

"Speak, then," Issah commanded, though Ra could see her tension in the rigid set of her shoulders.

Set's smile widened. "The Architect is more organized than you believe. They have cells throughout the city, funded by sources that would surprise you. And they have a patron—someone with resources to match their ambition, someone who wants to see this government fall."

"Who?" Kiya demanded.

Set turned to face Horus, and for a moment the chamber held its breath. "That, I cannot say with certainty. But I can say where the money flows. And I can say that someone in this room has been whispering in the dark, making promises they intend to break."

Horus's face remained impassive, but Ra saw his fingers tighten on the arm of his chair. "If you have accusations to make, brother of Ra, make them. Otherwise, cease this theater."

"I make no accusations," Set said, spreading his hands in a gesture of innocence that fooled no one. "I merely offer my services. I have resources, contacts, ways of learning secrets that your investigator cannot access. Together, we could find the killer before they strike again."

"And what would you ask in return?" Ra spoke for the first time, his voice rough from disuse. Every eye turned to him, and he felt the weight of their attention like a physical pressure.

Set's gaze met his, and Ra saw the old hatred there, mixed with something else—respect, perhaps, or calculation. "Only what is fair, brother. A seat on this council. A voice in the government I helped to create."

"You tried to destroy that government," Khenmu pointed out.

"I tried to preserve what was worth preserving," Set corrected. "We differed on methods, not goals. But now we face a common enemy. The Architect threatens us all—Shetu and human alike. Surely we can put aside old grievances to face this threat together?"

Ra looked at Issah, reading the conflict in her eyes. Set was dangerous, unpredictable, driven by ambition and wounded pride. But he was also right—the Architect was a threat that transcended old alliances. And if Set truly had information...

"We will consider your offer," Issah said carefully. "But any cooperation comes with conditions. You answer to Kiya. You share everything you learn. And if you betray us, there will be no second chances."

Set bowed, the gesture carrying a mocking edge. "As my queen commands."

The session dissolved into smaller conferences, factions breaking off to whisper and scheme. Ra remained where he sat, watching the political theater unfold around him. He had ruled for millennia, had shaped the destiny of nations, and now he was reduced to a spectator in his own drama.

"You shouldn't have spoken," Issah murmured, sliding onto the bench beside him. "Set will use your presence against you."

"He would have used my absence just as easily," Ra replied. "At least this way, he must address me directly."

"He wants the child dead, doesn't he?" Issah's hand moved to her stomach again, protective and fierce. "A hybrid represents everything Set hates—the blurring of boundaries, the mixing of bloodlines. He would see our baby as an abomination."

"Set sees everything as a threat or an opportunity," Ra said. "Our child might be both to him. The question is which he chooses to emphasize."

Across the chamber, Kiya was engaged in heated conversation with Meritamen, while Horus had gathered a cluster of Shetu delegates around him. Set stood alone, watching everything with the patience of a spider at the center of its web.

"We need to leave," Ra said quietly. "This chamber is a trap, and we're the bait."

Issah nodded, rising with the grace that had first drawn him to her. "Then let's not disappoint our hunters. The game has only begun."

As they made their way toward the side entrance, Ra felt eyes following them—some curious, some hostile, some impossible to read. The council had achieved nothing but to deepen the divisions, to sow more suspicion where trust was needed.

And somewhere in the city, the killer walked free, planning their next move while the government argued about procedure. Time was running out. The child grew within Issah, vulnerable and precious, and the threats multiplied like shadows at sunset.

Ra stepped out into the bright Egyptian day, feeling the sun on his face—Horus's sun now, not his. He was mortal, powerless, hunted. But he was also loved, and he was determined, and he would not let his family fall to the darkness gathering around them.

The council had failed. Now it was up to them.
