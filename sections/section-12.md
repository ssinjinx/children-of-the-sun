Thoth arrived on the seventh day, when Ra had regained enough strength to sit upright without dizziness. The god of wisdom came alone, without fanfare or escort, his ibis-headed form wrapped in simple linen rather than the ceremonial robes he usually favored.

"You look terrible," he said, settling onto the stool Issah offered. "I expected better from the former lord of the sun."

"I expected you sooner," Ra replied. "What kept you?"

"Research." Thoth's eyes gleamed with the particular light that came when he had discovered something new, something that expanded the boundaries of knowledge. "Your message mentioned a hybrid child, unprecedented in Shetu history. I needed to verify whether such a thing was even possible before I committed to involvement."

"And your conclusion?"

"It's not only possible—it's happened before." Thoth produced a scroll from his satchel, ancient and fragile, its edges crumbling. "In the time before time, when the Shetu first came to this world, there were... experiments. Attempts to merge divine essence with mortal flesh. Most failed. But some succeeded, in limited ways."

Issah leaned forward, her hand unconsciously moving to her belly. "What happened to them? The successful ones?"

"They became something new. Something the universe had not anticipated." Thoth unrolled the scroll, revealing diagrams that hurt to look at—geometries that seemed to shift and change, symbols that burned with hidden meaning. "The Shetu elders destroyed most of the records, fearing what such beings might become. But I found references in the oldest archives, hints of powers that transcended the boundaries between mortal and divine."

Ra felt a chill that had nothing to do with his weakened state. "Powers?"

"The ability to bridge worlds, to communicate across distances that should be impossible, to tap into energies that the Shetu themselves cannot access." Thoth's voice dropped to a whisper. "Your child, Ra, is not just a hybrid. It is a potential key. A bridge between what was and what could be."

"A key to what?"

Thoth looked at him, and for the first time, Ra saw fear in the god of wisdom's eyes. "To home. To the place the Shetu came from, before they became what they are now."

The room fell silent. Outside, the sounds of Memphis continued—vendors calling, children playing, life proceeding unaware of the revelation that had just shaken their world.

"The Shetu came from somewhere else," Thoth continued. "Not from this world, not from any world we know. They arrived here thousands of years ago, fleeing something, seeking refuge. The old stories speak of a great journey, a crossing between realms. But the way back was lost, the knowledge destroyed or hidden."

"And you think my child can find it?" Issah asked.

"I think your child might be able to receive a signal that has been broadcasting for millennia, waiting for a receiver capable of hearing it." Thoth produced another scroll, this one covered in mathematical notations that made Ra's head spin. "I've detected anomalies in the celestial patterns, energy signatures that don't match anything in our understanding of physics. They're coming from outside our solar system, from distances that should make communication impossible."

"A signal," Ra repeated. "From the Shetu's original home."

"Or from something else entirely." Thoth's expression was grave. "The signal is complex, layered, almost... intelligent. It responds to certain frequencies, certain conditions. I've been trying to find a way to amplify it, to make it strong enough to decode."

"And you need the child for this."

"I need a bridge." Thoth corrected. "Your child, if it develops as I suspect, might be able to serve as that bridge. Its unique biology could allow it to receive and translate the signal in ways that neither pure Shetu nor pure human could manage."

Ra felt the old anger rising, the protective fury of a father whose child was being discussed as a tool rather than a person. "You want to use my child as an antenna?"

"I want to use your child's unique capabilities to answer the greatest question in existence: where did we come from, and can we ever go back?" Thoth leaned forward, his intensity palpable. "Don't you understand, Ra? This could change everything. It could mean rescue for the Shetu, a way home for those who have been stranded here for millennia. It could mean answers to questions that have haunted us since the beginning."

"At what cost?" Issah asked quietly. "What would this... bridging... do to my child?"

Thoth hesitated, and that hesitation spoke volumes. "I don't know. The records are unclear. The process might be harmless, or it might... change the child. Alter its development, its nature, its very essence."

"No." Ra's voice was firm, despite his weakness. "You will not experiment on my child. Not now, not ever."

"Even if it means never knowing the truth? Even if it means the Shetu remain stranded here forever, slowly dying out as their bloodlines thin and their power fades?"

"Even then." Ra met Thoth's gaze, letting the god of wisdom see the determination in his eyes. "My child is not a tool for your research. It is a person, with its own life to live, its own choices to make. When it is old enough to understand, it can decide for itself whether to help you. But until then, you will leave it alone."

Thoth was silent for a long moment, his ibis head tilted in thought. Then, surprisingly, he smiled. "You have changed, Ra. The god I knew would have seized this opportunity without hesitation, would have sacrificed anything for knowledge."

"The god you knew didn't understand love," Ra said. "I do now."

"Perhaps that's why I came." Thoth stood, gathering his scrolls. "I will respect your wishes, Ra. I won't press the matter until the child is old enough to choose. But I will continue my research, continue searching for other ways to reach the signal. And when the time comes... I hope your child will be willing to help."

"When the time comes," Issah said, "we'll ask them. Not tell. Ask."

"Fair enough." Thoth moved to the door, then paused. "One more thing. The blood you need for the child—I can provide it. My divine essence is still strong, and I have no objection to sharing it. It might not be enough on its own, but combined with what Ra can safely give, it should sustain the pregnancy until birth."

Ra felt a surge of gratitude that made his eyes sting. "Thank you, old friend."

"Don't thank me yet." Thoth's expression was unreadable. "The birth will be difficult. The child is... different. Stronger than a human infant, more demanding than a Shetu. It will require constant care, constant feeding, constant attention. And there are those who will never accept it, who will see it as a threat to be eliminated rather than a miracle to be cherished."

"We'll protect it," Issah said. "Whatever it takes."

"I believe you will." Thoth opened the door, letting in the afternoon light. "That's why I'm helping. Not for the research, though that matters. But because some things are worth protecting, even at the cost of knowledge."

He left them there, in the small house that had become their fortress, with the revelation of the signal echoing in their minds. Ra looked at Issah, at the woman who had taught him what it meant to be human, and felt the weight of their situation settle over him like a blanket.

A signal from the stars. A child who might be the key to everything. Enemies gathering in the shadows, waiting for their moment to strike.

And in the middle of it all, a simple truth: they were a family, and they would fight for each other until the end.

"Whatever comes," he said, taking Issah's hand.

"Whatever comes," she agreed.

Outside, the sun continued its journey across the sky, indifferent to the dramas unfolding below. But somewhere in the darkness between stars, a signal pulsed, waiting, hoping for someone to hear.

And in a small house in Memphis, a child grew, unique in all the world, carrying within it the potential to change everything.
