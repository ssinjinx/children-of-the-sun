The morning sun cast long shadows across Memphis's limestone streets as Issah made her way to the council chambers. Where once she had slipped through these alleys like a ghost – a slave girl beneath notice – now the crowds parted before her. Some bowed their heads in deference, others watched with naked curiosity, and a few, she noted, quickly averted their eyes. She was no longer invisible, but neither was she fully seen. Instead, she had become a symbol: The Liberator, The Human Queen, The One Who Broke the Chains.

Her sandaled feet carried her past the market square where merchants were setting up their stalls. The scent of fresh bread and roasted figs mingled with incense from the temple district. A child pointed at her, mouth agape, before his mother quickly pulled him away. Issah forced herself to maintain her measured pace, though part of her longed to run and hide in the shadows as she once had.

"Morning blessings, my queen," called out an elderly potter, his hands still clay-stained from his work. Issah nodded, managing a small smile. Queen. The title still felt foreign, like borrowed clothing that didn't quite fit.

Two of her guards – both former resistance fighters – flanked her at a respectful distance. She had insisted on walking rather than taking a litter, despite her advisors' protests. "Let them see me," she had argued. "Let them know their leader walks the same streets they do." Besides, she had spent enough years watching the world through latticed windows and carved screens. Freedom meant feeling the earth beneath her feet.

As she approached the council chambers – a modest building of sandstone and cedar that had once been a merchant's villa – Issah's hand drifted unconsciously to her stomach. The slight swell was imperceptible beneath her linen robes, but she knew it was there. New life, unprecedented life, growing within her. Her secret felt like a flame she carried, warming and threatening all at once.

The guards at the entrance straightened as she approached. Inside, the villa's central courtyard had been converted into a gathering space, with benches arranged in a semicircle around a simple wooden chair – her seat. No golden throne, no elaborate dais. Those had been melted down or sold to feed the people during the lean months after the revolution.

"My queen." Khenmu, her chief advisor, hurried forward. His dark face was creased with worry. "The grain reports from Upper Egypt have arrived. And there are more incidents to discuss."

Issah nodded, already feeling the weight of the day settling onto her shoulders. This was the part of leadership no one had prepared her for. She had been trained as a spy, taught to gather secrets and strike from the shadows. Now she needed to build rather than destroy, to nurture rather than eliminate. Sometimes she wondered if Ra had known this would be her greatest challenge when he had supported her claim to leadership.

Ra. Her hand touched her stomach again, brief as a butterfly's landing. What would he say when she told him? Would those ancient eyes shine with joy or darken with concern? No Shetu had ever bred with a human – it was one of their most sacred laws. Yet here she was, carrying impossible life within her.

"Gather the council," she said, pushing aside her personal concerns. "Let's begin."

The council members filed in, taking their places on the benches. Humans and Shetu sat interspersed – a sight that would have been unthinkable just months ago. Issah studied their faces as they settled: Khenmu, her pragmatic chief advisor; Neferet, the sharp-minded granary overseer; Amenemhat, representing the merchant guilds; and Sekhet, one of the few remaining Shetu elders who had supported her claim to leadership.

'The grain situation first,' Issah commanded, keeping her voice steady. 'Neferet?'

The older woman stood, clutching a papyrus scroll. 'The floods came late this year, my queen. The harvest in Upper Egypt will be less than half what we need. Already, the price of bread has doubled in Thebes.' She paused, her weathered face grim. 'There are rumors of hoarding.'

'By whom?' Issah leaned forward.

'Some of the old noble families, those who still have private granaries. And...' Neferet hesitated. 'Some say the Shetu are taking more than their share.'

A low murmur rippled through the chamber. Sekhet's pale face remained impassive, but Issah saw his fingers tighten on his staff.

'The Shetu require little grain,' he said quietly. 'We sustain ourselves primarily through blood, as all know.'

'Which brings us to the next matter,' Khenmu interjected. 'The blood supply system is failing in the outer provinces. Three Shetu have died in the past month – simply faded away because they could not or would not feed.'

Issah felt the familiar ache in her chest. The voluntary blood donation system she had established – replacing the old practice of forced tribute – was meant to be the foundation of a new cooperation between humans and Shetu. But old fears died hard, and many humans still refused to participate.

'How many volunteers are currently registered?' she asked.

'Fewer than last month,' Khenmu replied. 'The temple priests have been spreading rumors that giving blood to the Shetu endangers one's journey to the afterlife.'

'Lies,' Sekhet hissed. 'We have existed alongside humans for millennia. The gods themselves—'

'The gods themselves are silent on this matter,' Issah cut in. 'We must solve this ourselves.' She turned to Amenemhat. 'What of the trade routes? Can we bring in grain from Syria?'

The merchant shifted uncomfortably. 'The caravans are being raided more frequently, my queen. The bandits seem to know exactly when and where to strike.' His eyes flicked briefly to Sekhet. 'Some suspect... inside information.'

More murmurs, darker this time. Issah raised her hand for silence.

'We cannot afford these suspicions,' she said firmly. 'Hunger and fear make enemies of friends. I want the guard patrols doubled on the trade routes. Neferet, audit all private granaries – any found hoarding will have their stores redistributed. Khenmu, work with the temple of Isis to counter these blood donation rumors. And Sekhet—'

'My queen.' A young guard burst into the chamber, face flushed. 'Forgive the interruption, but there's been an incident in the artisans' quarter.'

Issah felt the tension in the room spike. 'Speak.'

'A Shetu has been found dead. Not faded, but... murdered. Staked through the heart with a bronze blade.'

The council erupted in chaos. Sekhet rose, his usual composure cracking. 'This is what comes of your 'peaceful cooperation'!' he snarled. 'While we starve, they plot to destroy us!'

'Enough!' Issah's voice cut through the tumult. 'This council is adjourned. Khenmu, gather the city guard captains. Sekhet, stay – I need details about Shetu death rites. The rest of you, go. And speak of this to no one.'

As the chamber emptied, Issah felt the morning's nausea rising again. She pressed her fingers to her temples, waiting until the last footsteps faded before allowing herself a moment of weakness.

'My queen?' Khenmu lingered by the door. 'Shall I summon the guard captains now?'

'Give me a moment,' she said. 'I need to... think.'

Alone in her private chamber adjacent to the council room, Issah sank onto a wooden bench. Her hands went to her stomach, feeling the slight roundness there. What was growing inside her? The child of a human and a Shetu – would it need blood to survive? Would it be scorned by both races? And Ra... she still hadn't told Ra.

The morning sun slanted through the narrow window, warming her face. She thought of the dead Shetu in the artisans' quarter, of the failing blood donation system, of the hungry children in Thebes. She had fought so hard for this peace, had sacrificed so much. Now it felt like sand slipping through her fingers.

A sharp knock at the door made her straighten. 'Enter.'

The same young guard appeared, even more agitated than before. 'My queen, forgive me, but there's more. The dead Shetu... it was Amenra.'

Issah felt the blood drain from her face. Amenra – one of Ra's closest advisors, and one of the few Shetu who had supported human liberation from the beginning.

'There's something else,' the guard continued, his voice dropping. 'Carved into his chest... a message. It says, 'The half-blood abomination will never rule.''

Issah's hands clutched her stomach protectively before she could stop herself. The guard's eyes widened as understanding dawned on his face.

'My queen... are you...?'

'Get Ra,' she cut him off. 'Now. And tell no one what you've seen here.' As the guard hurried away, Issah rose and moved to the window. Below, she could see the city stirring to life, unaware of the storm gathering around them. Her child – Ra's child – moved within her, a flutter so faint she might have imagined it.

Someone knew her secret. Someone wanted her child dead before it was even born. And they were willing to kill to prevent a half-blood from existing.

The peace she had built was balanced on a knife's edge, and now that balance was tipping toward chaos. As she watched the morning shadows retreat across the limestone streets, Issah made a silent vow to her unborn child: I fought to free this city once. I will fight again to make it safe for you.

But first, she had a murder to solve, and a secret to share with its father.
