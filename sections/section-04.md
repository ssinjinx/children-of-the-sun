The council chambers reeked of copper and incense, an unholy marriage that made Issah's stomach turn. She pressed a hand against the swell of her belly, now barely visible beneath her linen dress, as if to shield their unborn child from the horror before them. The morning sun, usually a comfort, cast accusatory fingers through the high windows, illuminating dust motes that danced above the bloodstain on the floor.

Ra stood motionless beside her, his mortal frame taut with a tension she recognized all too well. In moments like these, she could still see the god he'd been—the raw power barely contained beneath his human skin. But there was something else now: vulnerability. Fear.

"The message was written in his blood," said Kiya, the investigator who'd met them at the chamber entrance. She was a slight woman with sharp eyes and a sharper tongue, her dark hair bound in tight braids against her scalp. She wore the leather jerkin and loose trousers of a street investigator rather than the ceremonial robes of palace authority, and she carried a bronze knife at her hip with the ease of someone who knew how to use it. "Whoever did this wanted you to see it, wanted you to know it wasn't just about Amenra."

The words stretched across the limestone wall in crude, dripping letters: 'The sun's legacy ends here. The child will never see the light.' Issah felt Ra's hand find hers, squeezing perhaps harder than he intended. His palm was slick with sweat—another reminder of his mortality, these small human tells he hadn't quite learned to master.

"Show me where you found him," Ra said, his voice hollow.

Kiya led them to the center of the chamber, where a dark stain had settled into the stone floor. The killer had arranged Amenra's body with deliberate care: arms outstretched, ankles crossed, a mockery of the sacred poses in temple paintings. But the violence of the act was evident in the spray patterns on nearby columns, in the overturned braziers and scattered scrolls.

"The wounds were precise," Kiya explained, crouching beside the bloodstain. She ran her finger along the edge of the stain, her eyes narrowing as she studied patterns invisible to Issah's untrained gaze. "Whoever did this knew exactly where to cut to make it slow. They wanted him to suffer, but they also wanted him alive long enough to write that message. The blade entered here, just below the ribs, angling upward to miss the vital organs. A professional's strike."

Issah felt bile rise in her throat. She'd seen death before—had caused it, even, during the rebellion—but this was different. This was intimately cruel, a warning wrapped in sacrifice. The copper smell seemed to intensify, clinging to the back of her throat, and she had to swallow hard to keep from retching.

"The Architect claims responsibility," Kiya continued, standing and dusting her hands against her trousers, "but something doesn't add up. Their previous attacks were crude, public. Bombs in marketplaces, poisoned wells. This..." She gestured to the scene around them, her sharp gaze taking in every detail: the carefully arranged body, the precise message, the location deep within the most secure building in Memphis. "This required access to the council chambers, knowledge of Amenra's schedule, an understanding of Shetu physiology. This was personal."

Ra moved to one of the columns, running his fingers over the blood spatter. His hand trembled slightly, and Issah noticed how he pressed his palm against the stone for support, as if the weight of what he saw was physically bearing down on him. In his mortal form, he couldn't read the memories in the blood as he once could, couldn't trace the echo of violence back to its source. The frustration showed in the tight set of his jaw, the way his shoulders bowed forward as if carrying an invisible burden.

"Someone helped them," he said, his voice barely above a whisper. He turned to face them, and Issah saw how age had begun to mark him—fine lines around his eyes, threads of silver in his beard that hadn't been there two years ago. Mortality was claiming him piece by piece. "Someone from within."

"My thoughts exactly," Kiya replied, her tone carrying a hint of respect for his insight. She pulled a small wax tablet from her satchel and began making notes with a stylus. "The question is: who among the Shetu would betray their own? And why target Amenra specifically? He was one of the few who supported the transition, who believed in peaceful coexistence."

Issah moved carefully through the chamber, her sandals whispering against the stone. The air felt thick, oppressive, as if the violence had left a tangible residue that clung to her skin. She paused at Amenra's desk, noting the careful arrangement of scrolls, the half-filled cup of wine now crusted brown at the bottom. A small oil lamp sat cold beside it, the wick trimmed short—a detail that seemed wrong, though she couldn't say why.

"He was working late," she said, picking up a stylus that had rolled to the edge of the desk. The weight of it felt suddenly heavy in her hand, an object that had been held by a man now dead. "These notes—they're about the child. About precedents in Shetu history, cases of..." She swallowed hard, the reality of their situation pressing against her like a physical weight. "Cases of hybridization."

Ra was at her side in an instant, his eyes scanning the hieroglyphs with the practiced ease of someone who had read them for millennia. His finger traced the characters, and she saw him mouthing the words silently, his expression shifting from curiosity to alarm.

"He was trying to help us," Ra said, his voice thick with emotion. "To understand what might happen when the baby... when our child is born. He found references in the oldest texts, fragments about unions between Shetu and humans from before the great laws were written."

"When your impossible child is born," Kiya finished, her tone carefully neutral. She looked between them, her sharp eyes assessing. "It seems Amenra's research made someone nervous. Someone who didn't want him—or you—to understand what this child might become."

The words hung in the air, heavy with implication. Issah set down the stylus and moved to the window, needing to feel the sun on her face, needing to escape the copper-thick air of death. Below, the city of Memphis went about its morning business—vendors hawking bread, children chasing dogs, life continuing indifferent to the violence above. She placed her hand on her stomach, feeling the slight swell that held their future, their hope, their vulnerability.

"What else did you find?" Ra asked Kiya, his voice regaining some of its authority. He was trying to be the leader he had once been, Issah realized, trying to set aside his grief and fear to solve the puzzle before them. But she could see the cost of that effort in the pallor of his skin, in the way he gripped the desk for support.

Kiya moved to the wall where the message had been written, studying the dried blood with a critical eye. "The writing shows hesitation here, at the beginning of the message. The killer's hand was steady at the end—they had found their rhythm, their conviction. But the first few letters are shaky. Whoever wrote this didn't start with confidence."

"Someone who knew Amenra," Issah said, turning from the window. "Someone who had to convince themselves to do this."

"Or someone who had never killed before," Kiya added. "The precision of the wound suggests training, but the psychological markers suggest a novice. It's a contradiction I can't resolve."

Ra moved to the doorway, examining the frame. "The guards at the entrance—what did they report?"

"Nothing," Kiya said, her voice carrying a note of frustration. "The night watch claims they saw no one enter after the council session ended. The side entrance, the one used by servants, was found unlatched this morning. But the tracks in the garden outside were deliberately obscured."

"A ghost, then," Ra murmured.

"Or someone the guards knew well enough not to question," Kiya countered. "Someone who belonged here."

The implication settled over them like a shroud. If the killer was one of their own, if the threat came from within the palace itself, then nowhere was safe. The walls that should protect them had become the walls of a trap.

"I need to see the body," Ra said suddenly. "Before they prepare him for burial."

Kiya hesitated, her sharp eyes studying his face. "Are you sure? It's not... it won't be easy."

"He was my friend for two hundred years," Ra said, his voice steady despite the grief that flickered in his eyes. "I owe him the truth. And perhaps his body will tell me what his murderer wouldn't."

They descended to the preparation chambers below the palace, a place of cool shadows and the faint scent of natron. The corridors grew narrower as they went deeper, the stone walls sweating with the moisture of the underground. Issah found herself walking close to Ra, her hand brushing against his, drawing comfort from his presence even as she tried to offer him hers.

Amenra's body lay on a stone slab, covered with a linen sheet. The priest who attended him bowed deeply as they entered, then retreated to a corner, his face carefully neutral. This was not a place for the living, and they were trespassing on sacred ground.

Ra pulled back the sheet slowly, revealing the face of his friend. Amenra had been ancient by Shetu standards, his skin like parchment stretched over delicate bones, his hair white as unbleached linen. In death, he looked smaller than Issah remembered, diminished not just by the absence of life but by the violence of his ending.

"The wound," Kiya said, pointing to the gaping hole in Amenra's chest. "Bronze blade, narrow, designed for precise work. See how the edges are clean? This wasn't a weapon of opportunity. This was chosen specifically for this task."

Ra leaned close, his nose wrinkling at the smell of death. Issah watched him study the wound, his expression shifting through grief, anger, and something she couldn't quite identify. Recognition, perhaps, or memory.

"Shetu anatomy is different from human," he said quietly. "The heart sits lower, protected by a cage of cartilage that human blades would glance off. To strike true, the killer needed to know exactly where to aim."

"Could a human have learned this?" Kiya asked.

Ra considered, his fingers hovering over the wound without touching it. "Possibly, with study. But it would take months of research, access to Shetu medical texts. Or..." He paused, his jaw tightening. "Or they were taught by someone who knew."

"A Shetu teaching humans how to kill Shetu," Kiya said, making another note on her tablet. "That's a dark thought."

"These are dark times," Ra replied.

Issah found her gaze drawn to Amenra's hands, folded across his chest in the traditional pose of rest. But there was something wrong with the fingers, something that made her step closer despite her revulsion.

"His nails," she said, her voice barely audible. "They're torn. And there... is that wax under them?"

Kiya was beside her instantly, producing a small bronze tool to gently scrape beneath Amenra's nail. She examined the residue, then held it to her nose, her eyes widening.

"Sealing wax," she said. "The kind used on official correspondence. And look—the nail is cracked here, as if he gripped something hard. A struggle, perhaps?"

"Or a message he tried to protect," Ra said, his voice quickening. "Check his robes. Check everywhere he might have hidden something."

They searched the body with careful urgency, the priest looking away as they violated the sanctity of preparation. It was Issah who found it, tucked into the lining of Amenra's belt—a small scrap of papyrus, folded and refolded until it was no larger than a fingernail.

She unfolded it with trembling fingers, revealing three words written in Amenra's precise hand: 'The falcon watches.'

"Falcon," Ra breathed, his face going pale. "Horus."

"The new sun god?" Kiya asked, her voice sharp with skepticism. "He's a boy, barely sixty years by Shetu reckoning. Surely he wouldn't—"

"Horus has always been ambitious," Ra interrupted, his voice hardening. "Even as a child, he chafed at my authority, resented that the sun's power passed to me rather than to him. When I gave up my godhood, he saw it as his chance to claim what he believed was his birthright."

"But to kill for it?" Issah asked, though even as she spoke, she remembered the coldness in Horus's eyes when they had last met, the barely concealed contempt with which he regarded her humanity.

"Power is a drug," Kiya said, "and Horus has tasted it for the first time. Perhaps he found it not to his liking, or perhaps he fears what your child might become. A hybrid with claims to both worlds could threaten everything he's built."

Ra turned away from the body, his shoulders heaving as he struggled to control his emotions. When he turned back, his face was a mask of determination, but Issah could see the cracks—the grief for his friend, the fear for his family, the crushing weight of a mortality that meant he couldn't simply smite his enemies with divine fire.

"We need proof," he said. "Suspicion isn't enough, especially against a god. We need evidence that will stand before the council, before the people."

"I'll find it," Kiya promised, her sharp eyes gleaming with the challenge. "But you'll need to be careful. If Horus is involved, he has resources we can't imagine. Eyes everywhere, ears in every wall."

"Then we'll be ghosts," Issah said, surprising herself with the steel in her voice. "We've hidden before, survived before. We'll do it again."

Ra looked at her, and she saw something shift in his expression—pride, love, and a fierce determination that mirrored her own. "Together," he said, echoing the word that had become their vow.

"Together," she agreed.

Kiya watched them, her sharp face unreadable. Then she tucked her wax tablet away and moved toward the door. "I'll start with Horus's household. Servants talk, if you know how to ask. In the meantime, keep to public spaces, vary your routes, trust no one."

"Not even you?" Issah asked, a ghost of her old sharpness returning.

Kiya paused at the door, a faint smile touching her lips. "Especially not me. But for what it's worth, I have no love for gods who think themselves above justice. Your child deserves a chance to be born, whatever it might be. I'll help you give it that chance."

She disappeared into the corridor, her footsteps fading into the stone. Issah turned to Ra, to the body of their friend, to the mystery that had become their life. The copper smell still clung to everything, but beneath it, she detected something else—the sharp scent of purpose, of war, of a mother's determination to protect her young.

"Come," Ra said, taking her hand. "We need to get you away from here. If Horus is truly behind this, then nowhere in Memphis is safe."

They left Amenra to the priests, climbing back toward the light. But Issah knew that the darkness had followed them, would always follow them, until they found the truth and faced it together.

The child within her stirred—a flutter so faint she might have imagined it. But she hadn't. She pressed her hand to her stomach, feeling the life that depended on their strength, their courage, their love.

First blood had been spilled. It would not be the last.
