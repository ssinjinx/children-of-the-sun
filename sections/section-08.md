The scroll sat on the table between them like a coiled serpent, its wax seal the color of dried blood, its surface innocent of any markings that might reveal its contents. Ra had placed it there an hour ago, after returning from the desert, and neither he nor Issah had touched it since.

"It could be a trap," Issah said finally, her voice hushed despite the fact that they were alone in their small house. "Everything Set does is calculated. This could be his way of getting close enough to strike."

"It could be," Ra agreed. He sat across from her, his hands wrapped around a cup of wine that had gone cold. "Or it could be exactly what he said—an attempt at redemption, or at least alliance."

"You want to believe him."

It wasn't an accusation, but it felt like one. Ra looked up at his wife, at the woman who had given up everything to be with him, and saw the fear she tried so hard to hide. Her belly was more pronounced now, rounding beneath her linen dress, a visible reminder of what they stood to lose.

"I want to stop the people who threaten our child," Ra said carefully. "If Set can help us do that, then yes, I want to believe him. But wanting and doing are different things."

Issah reached out and took the scroll, breaking the seal with a decisive motion. The wax crumbled like old bone, scattering across the table. She unrolled the papyrus, revealing lines of hieroglyphs that seemed to shift and move in the lamplight.

"It's ciphered," she said, her brow furrowing. "Not a simple substitution either. This looks like..." She traced the symbols with her finger, her lips moving silently. "This is old. Predynastic, maybe. The kind of code priests used to keep secrets from the pharaohs."

"Can you read it?"

"Not without the key." Issah looked up at him, her eyes sharp with the intelligence that had made her a leader. "But Kiya might know someone who can. Or Thoth. If anyone remembers predynastic ciphers, it would be the god of wisdom."

Ra felt the familiar pang of loss at the mention of his former peer. Thoth had been his friend once, before the revolution, before everything changed. Now they moved in different circles, the god of wisdom serving Horus while Ra served mortality.

"We can't take this to Thoth," he said. "Not yet. If Set is telling the truth, there are spies everywhere. We need to be careful who we trust."

"Then what do we do?"

Ra took the scroll from her, studying the symbols. They seemed familiar, hovering at the edge of memory like a dream half-forgotten. He had lived through the predynastic era, had walked among those ancient peoples before the unification of the Two Lands. Perhaps...

"Give me time," he said. "Let me try to remember."

He spent the night at the table, candles burning low as he worked through the cipher, trying to summon memories from eight thousand years of existence. The symbols danced before his eyes, shifting between familiar and strange, sometimes resolving into meaning only to dissolve again into nonsense.

Issah went to bed eventually, her body demanding rest that his mortal form no longer required. He listened to her breathing shift into sleep, and felt a fierce protective surge that sharpened his focus.

By dawn, he had it. Not the full message—his memory was imperfect, damaged by his transformation—but enough to understand the pattern. Enough to read the first name.

Khufu.

Meritamen's secretary. A man who had access to every meeting, every decision, every secret of the council. If he was compromised, then nothing was safe.

Ra sat back, his eyes burning from strain, his mind racing. One name confirmed. Two more to decode. And somewhere in the city, a third traitor whose identity Set had only hinted at.

He woke Issah with tea and bread, and together they studied what he had learned.

"Khufu," she said, tasting the name. "I've met him. Quiet, efficient, always in the background. He could be the source of the leaks Kiya identified."

"We need to confirm it before we act," Ra said. "If we accuse him wrongly, we destroy an innocent man and alert the real traitors."

"And if we wait too long?"

"Then they move against us before we're ready."

The choice sat heavy between them. They had faced impossible decisions before, but never with so much at stake, never with their child's future hanging in the balance.

"Kiya," Issah said finally. "We tell Kiya what we've learned, let her investigate quietly. She's careful, methodical. She won't move until she's certain."

"And Set's other names?"

"We decode them first." Issah took his hands in hers, her grip firm and warm. "We don't act on half-information. We do this right, or we don't do it at all."

It took two more days of intense work, Ra delving deep into memories he had thought lost, Issah cross-referencing symbols in the few old texts they possessed. The second name emerged slowly: Anhert, a temple priest who administered the blood tithes, who knew which Shetu were feeding regularly and which were vulnerable to starvation.

"If he's controlling the blood supply," Issah said, her face pale, "he could be responsible for the Shetu deaths Khenmu mentioned. Killing them slowly, making it look like natural starvation."

"Or using the threat of starvation to force cooperation," Ra added. "Control the blood, control the Shetu."

The third name remained stubbornly encoded, the cipher shifting in ways that defied Ra's attempts at translation. But they had enough to act, enough to give Kiya a direction for her investigation.

They met her at the same abandoned temple, the moon once again their only witness. Ra showed her what they had decoded, watching her face as she absorbed the implications.

"Khufu," she said slowly. "Yes. He has access, opportunity, motive. He's Meritamen's cousin by marriage—if she's compromised, he would be the conduit."

"And Anhert?"

"I've suspected the temple administration for weeks. The blood supply irregularities are too systematic to be accidental." Kiya's eyes were bright with the hunter's excitement. "If Set is telling the truth..."

"If," Ra emphasized.

"If," she agreed. "Then we're looking at the outer layers of something much larger. The Children of Darkness don't expose their core members easily. These are tools, not leaders."

"Can you investigate without alerting them?"

Kiya smiled, sharp and predatory. "I've spent fifteen years learning to be invisible. Give me three days. I'll have evidence, or I'll have confirmation that Set is playing us."

"Three days," Issah said. "That's all we have. Set said they would move against us in three days."

"Then I'll work fast." Kiya gathered the notes, tucking them into her leather satchel. "In the meantime, be careful. Khufu and Anhert aren't the only threats. If there is a third traitor closer to you..."

"We'll watch everyone," Ra said. "Trust no one."

"Not even me," Kiya agreed. "Verification is the only protection against betrayal."

She disappeared into the night as silently as she had come, leaving Ra and Issah alone in the temple's ruins.

"We chose this path," Issah said softly. "The investigation. The secrets. The constant watching."

"We chose to protect our child," Ra corrected. "This is what that protection requires."

They walked home through the sleeping city, keeping to shadows, changing routes to avoid patterns. Every face seemed suspicious, every shadow potentially hid a knife. The paranoia that Set had warned them about was taking root in their own hearts.

But as they reached their house and bolted the door behind them, Ra felt something else alongside the fear: determination. They had a path forward now, names to investigate, leads to follow. For the first time since Amenra's murder, they were not simply reacting to events. They were taking control.

Issah pressed her hand to her belly, feeling for movement. "Three days," she whispered. "Whatever happens, we face it together."

"Together," Ra agreed.

Outside, the sun was rising, painting the sky in shades of gold and rose. A new day, full of danger and possibility. And somewhere in the city, the Children of Darkness counted down the hours until their plans would unfold.

The game had begun in earnest. And Ra was determined to win.
