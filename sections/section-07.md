The desert wind carried the scent of dust and distant rain as Ra made his way through the outer districts of Memphis, his footsteps kicking up small clouds with each stride. Set had sent a message—cryptic as always—requesting a meeting at an old waystation beyond the city walls. Against his better judgment, and Issah's vehement protests, Ra had agreed.

"You can't trust him," she had said that morning, her hands clasped over her belly in the gesture that had become habitual. "He's your brother, yes, but he's also the one who tried to kill you. Who tried to destroy everything we built."

"I don't trust him," Ra had replied, gathering his cloak against the morning chill. "But he has information we need. And if there's even a chance he can help us find Amenra's killer..."

He had left before she could argue further, before the tears gathering in her eyes could weaken his resolve. It was easier to be strong when he didn't have to face her fear.

The waystation was a crumbling structure of mud brick and timber, abandoned when the new trade routes shifted eastward. It hunched against the desert like a wounded animal, its roof partially collapsed, its courtyard choked with sand. Set waited in the shade of the remaining overhang, dressed in crimson linen that seemed to absorb the morning light.

"Brother," Set said, his voice carrying that particular tone of mockery that Ra remembered from their childhood. "You came alone. I'm impressed. Or perhaps disappointed. I expected your human queen to chain you to her side."

"Issah is protecting our child," Ra said, stopping just beyond arm's reach. Old instincts died hard, and he had learned long ago never to let Set within striking distance. "The child you would see as an abomination."

Set's smile was sharp as broken glass. "Would I? You assume much, Ra. Perhaps too much."

"Then tell me what you want."

Set gestured to a rough bench against the wall, taking a seat himself with the casual grace that had always marked him. Reluctantly, Ra sat as well, keeping his weight balanced, ready to move if necessary.

"I want what I've always wanted," Set said, producing a wineskin and drinking deeply before offering it to Ra. "Order. Stability. A world where the strong rule and the weak serve. The revolution you supported destroyed all of that, replaced it with chaos and uncertainty."

"And yet you said you would help us."

"I said I would help you find a killer." Set's eyes, dark as desert shadows, met Ra's. "I didn't say I had changed my nature."

The silence between them stretched, filled with decades of resentment and blood. They had been close once, before the jealousy took root, before Set's ambition outgrew his place. Ra remembered a time when they had hunted together in the celestial fields, when Set's laughter had been genuine rather than weaponized.

"Tell me about the Children of Darkness," Ra said finally.

Set's expression flickered—surprise, quickly hidden. "You've learned their name. Kiya has been busy."

"She says they've infiltrated the government. That they want my child dead."

"Your child represents change," Set said, his voice dropping to something almost thoughtful. "Change is always threatening to those who benefit from stasis. The Children of Darkness have existed for centuries, Ra. They predate your reign, predate the Shetu themselves if the oldest stories are true."

"What do they want?"

"To maintain the balance." Set drank again from the wineskin, his gaze fixed on the distant horizon where heat ripples distorted the air. "They believe that the divine and mortal must remain separate, that mixing the bloodlines leads to corruption and chaos. Your child—the first true hybrid—represents the breaking of that balance."

Ra felt a chill despite the warming air. "And you? What do you believe?"

Set was quiet for a long moment, his fingers tracing patterns in the sand at his feet. When he spoke, his voice was different—stripped of mockery, stripped of artifice. "I believe that power flows where it will, brother. I believe that trying to contain it is like trying to cup water in your hands."

"That doesn't answer my question."

"No," Set agreed, smiling again. "It doesn't."

He stood abruptly, moving to the edge of the overhang and staring out at the desert. "I can give you names. Three members of the Children of Darkness operating within the city. High-level, trusted, unsuspected. With them in custody, you could begin to dismantle the organization."

"And in exchange?"

Set turned, and Ra saw something in his brother's face that he hadn't expected—weariness, perhaps, or loneliness. "A place in your child's life. Not power, not authority. Just... presence."

"You tried to destroy me."

"I tried to become you. There's a difference." Set moved closer, close enough that Ra could smell the wine on his breath, the desert spices in his hair. "I failed. The throne passed to Horus rather than me, and I've had years to consider my errors."

"Errors?"

"Tactics, then. Strategy." Set's hand came up to grip Ra's shoulder, and Ra forced himself not to flinch. "I thought power was something you took by force. But you—you gave it away, brother. You surrendered everything to be with your human queen. And somehow, impossibly, that made you stronger."

"I made a choice."

"You made a sacrifice. And that sacrifice has consequences." Set's grip tightened. "The Children of Darkness fear what your child represents not because it breaks the old rules, but because it creates new ones. A hybrid with divine heritage could challenge the very foundations of our society. Could remake the world."

"Or be killed before it draws breath," Ra said quietly.

"Yes." Set released his shoulder and stepped back. "Which is why you need me. I know how they think, Ra. I know how they operate. And I know how to stop them."

Ra studied his brother's face, searching for deception, for the tells that had always given Set away in their games of strategy. But he saw only determination, and something that might have been regret.

"Three names," Ra said. "Give me three names, and I'll consider your offer."

Set smiled, and this time there was no mockery in it. "Always bargaining, brother. Very well. The first is Meritamen's secretary, a man named Khufu. The second is a temple priest named Anhert who administers the blood tithes. The third..." He paused, letting the tension build. "The third is someone you trust. Someone close to the queen. I won't speak their name here, but I will tell you how to identify them."

"How?"

Set leaned close, his voice dropping to a whisper. "They wear a scarab of lapis and gold, a gift from the old regime. And they meet their contacts in the garden of the palace of the rising sun, every night when the evening star appears."

Ra felt the words hit him like physical blows. The description was specific enough to identify someone, but vague enough to apply to multiple people in Issah's inner circle. Set was playing his game, doling out truth mixed with enough uncertainty to maintain his leverage.

"Why should I believe you?"

"Because I'm telling the truth," Set said simply. "For once, brother, I'm telling the truth."

He produced a small scroll from his robe, sealed with wax the color of dried blood. "The names are here, written in cipher. Break it if you can, or bring it to your investigator friend. She seems capable enough."

Ra took the scroll, feeling the weight of it in his palm. "If you're lying..."

"Then you'll have learned nothing new. But if I'm telling the truth..." Set's smile returned, softer now. "Then perhaps we can begin to repair what was broken between us."

He turned and walked toward the desert, his crimson robes catching the wind like wings. "Three days, Ra. That's how long you have before the Children of Darkness move against the queen. Use them wisely."

"Set."

His brother paused at the edge of the waystation, looking back.

"Why help us? Truly?"

Set was silent for a moment, his silhouette dark against the brightening sky. When he spoke, his voice carried none of its usual edge. "Because you chose love over power, brother. And I've spent too long choosing the opposite. Perhaps... perhaps it's time I learned from your example."

Then he was gone, swallowed by the heat ripples and blowing sand, leaving Ra alone with a scroll that might contain salvation or destruction, and the weight of a brother's unexpected honesty.

Ra sat on the bench for a long time after Set disappeared, watching the desert shimmer in the morning heat. He thought of all the times they had been enemies, all the blood spilled between them, all the words that could never be taken back. And he thought of Set's final words, wondering if they could possibly be true.

In the end, he tucked the scroll into his cloak and began the walk back to Memphis. Whatever Set's true motives, the threat was real. And Ra would do whatever was necessary to protect his family—even if it meant trusting the one person he had sworn never to trust again.

The desert wind followed him home, carrying whispers of sand and secrets, of old betrayals and possible redemptions. And somewhere in the city, the Children of Darkness moved like shadows, counting down the hours until their plans came to fruition.

Three days.

It wasn't enough time. But it was all they had.
