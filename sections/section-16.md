Set moved with the patience of a spider in its web, feeling the vibrations of approaching prey. The government had fractured beautifully—exactly as he had predicted, though he would never admit to Horus that he had known the betrayal would cause such chaos. Let the sun god believe he was merely opportunistic; the truth was more complex.

In his desert fortress, surrounded by those Shetu who had never accepted the revolution's outcome, Set prepared his move. The Children of Darkness were fools, puppets dancing on strings they couldn't see. The real power lay not in their ancient prejudices but in the vacuum they created—chaos that needed a strong hand to tame.

"The hardliners are ready," his lieutenant reported, a scarred veteran named Khenti who had served him for centuries. "They await your command."

"And the others? The moderates who still believe in cooperation?"

"Confused. Frightened. Looking for leadership."

Set smiled. "Then let us provide it."

His coup began not with violence but with words—speeches in the Shetu districts, promises of protection, warnings about human betrayal. The message spread through networks older than the revolution: the humans had proven they couldn't be trusted. The transitional government was a failure. Only strength could preserve Shetu culture, Shetu identity, Shetu survival.

By the time the first blood was spilled, it was almost an afterthought—a human merchant killed in a "misunderstanding," a Shetu elder who spoke against the new movement found drowned in the Nile. Set condemned the violence publicly while privately ensuring it continued, building the pressure that would force his people to choose sides.

Ra learned of the coup as it was happening, reports flooding into the fractured council from districts across the city. Shetu hardliners had seized the granaries, the blood banks, the communication centers. The transitional government's authority was evaporating by the hour.

"He's been planning this for months," Kiya said, studying the maps that showed Set's territory expanding like a stain. "Since before Sekhet was exposed. Maybe since the beginning."

"Set always plans," Ra replied, his voice heavy with memory. "When we were children, he would calculate ten moves ahead while I was still deciding my first. It's what made him dangerous then, and what makes him dangerous now."

"Can we stop him?"

"Not with the council in its current state. Not with humans and Shetu at each other's throats." Ra stood, moving to the window where the afternoon sun cast long shadows across the city. "We need allies. We need to show that cooperation is still possible, that Set's way isn't the only way."

"And how do we do that?"

"By winning." Ra turned back to face them, and Issah saw something in his eyes that she hadn't seen since before the pregnancy, since before the weakness—the fire of the god he had been. "Set understands strength. He respects it. If we can defeat him, prove that his path leads only to destruction, then the hardliners will have no choice but to return to the table."

"Defeat a god?" Meritamen asked, her voice incredulous. "With what army?"

"Not an army. An idea." Ra moved to Issah, taking her hands in his. "Set believes that humans and Shetu cannot coexist, that the mixing of bloodlines is weakness. We prove him wrong by showing that our union—your leadership, my support, our child—is stronger than his division."

"He'll kill you," Issah said quietly. "If he sees you as a threat, he'll end you without hesitation."

"Then I won't be a threat. I'll be an alternative."

The plan that emerged was desperate, perhaps impossible, but it was all they had. Ra would go to Set, not as an enemy but as a brother, offering one last chance at reconciliation before the conflict became total. While he distracted Set with words, Kiya and her network would undermine the hardliners' logistical support, cutting off their resources and isolating their leadership.

"It's a gamble," Kiya observed.

"Everything is a gamble now." Ra gathered his cloak, preparing to step into danger once more. "But if we succeed, if we can stop this before it becomes open war, then we buy the time we need. Time for the child to be born. Time for the new council to establish itself. Time for hope to take root."

"And if you fail?"

"Then you run." He met Issah's eyes, holding them with all the love he possessed. "Take the child, go where Set can't find you. Live. Build the future without me."

"I won't—"

"Promise me." His voice was fierce. "Promise me that no matter what happens, you'll survive. For the child. For the future."

She held his gaze for a long moment, then nodded slowly. "I promise."

It was a lie, and they both knew it. If Ra fell, she would fall beside him, fighting to the end. But the lie gave him peace as he walked out into the city, toward the confrontation that would decide everything.

Set received him in the old palace, sitting on a throne that had once been Ra's own. The symbolism was intentional, cruel, perfect.

"Brother," Set said, his voice carrying no hint of surprise. "I wondered when you would come."

"To stop you."

"Or to join me?" Set's smile was sharp. "You see what's happening out there. The dream is dying, Ra. The revolution has failed. I'm simply offering the Shetu a future that doesn't involve extinction."

"By turning back the clock? By reestablishing the very oppression that caused the revolution in the first place?"

"By establishing order." Set stood, descending the dais to stand before his brother. "Humans and Shetu cannot coexist as equals. It's been tried, and it has failed. The only question now is whether we manage the separation peacefully or through bloodshed."

"There's a third option." Ra held his ground, though every instinct screamed to retreat from the power radiating from his brother. "We find a new way. We build something that has never existed before."

"With your child? Your abomination?" Set's voice hardened. "Even you must see the danger, Ra. A being that is neither human nor Shetu, that could upset the balance of both worlds. Is that really the legacy you want to leave?"

"It's the only legacy worth having."

They stared at each other, brothers and enemies, the past and possible futures weighing between them.

"Then we are at an impasse," Set said finally. "I cannot stop what I've started, and you cannot allow it to continue."

"No." Ra's hand moved to the knife at his belt—not to draw it, but to remind Set that even gods could bleed. "But we can choose how it ends."

The confrontation that followed would be remembered for generations, though not in the way either brother expected. Not as a battle of divine power, but as something simpler, more human—a test of wills, a clash of visions, two brothers fighting for the soul of their world.

And somewhere in the city, the child moved in its mother's womb, sensing the conflict, waiting to be born into a world that desperately needed what only it could provide.
