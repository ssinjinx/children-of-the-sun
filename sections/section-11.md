The weakness crept up on Ra like tide upon sand—gradual, inexorable, impossible to resist. For three weeks, he had given his blood to sustain the child, watching Sekhet draw the dark red liquid from his veins and transfer it to Issah's waiting body. Three weeks of watching the color drain from his skin, the strength from his muscles, the clarity from his mind.

He told himself it was temporary. Told himself that Thoth would arrive soon with additional donors, that the burden would be shared, that he could recover. But Thoth had not come, and the weakness deepened with each passing day.

It happened on a morning like any other. Ra had risen before dawn, as was his habit, intending to fetch water for the day's needs. The clay jar was heavy, but he had carried heavier burdens. He made it to the doorway before his knees buckled.

The jar shattered against the stone floor, sending water across the packed earth like a sudden spring. Ra followed it down, his vision tunneling, his heart hammering against ribs that suddenly felt too fragile to contain it.

"Ra!"

Issah's voice came from far away, muffled by the rushing sound in his ears. He felt her hands on his face, her panic like heat against his skin.

"I'm fine," he tried to say, but the words came out slurred, meaningless.

"Sekhet! Someone, help!"

The healer arrived with speed that suggested she had been expecting this. Her cool hands checked his pulse, his temperature, the pallor of his lips. Ra watched her face through a haze of gray, saw the concern in her ancient eyes.

"He's given too much," she said. "The mortal form wasn't meant to sustain this level of donation."

"Will he die?" Issah's voice was tight with controlled terror.

"Not if we stop now. But he needs rest, nourishment, time to recover his strength."

"The child..."

"Will need alternative sources." Sekhet's face entered Ra's field of vision, filling the gray world with her severe features. "Can you hear me, Ra?"

He managed a nod, the movement costing him more energy than he had to spare.

"No more donations. Not until you're stronger. If you continue at this rate, you'll die before the child is born."

"Rather die... than let them..." The words cost him, each one a small exhaustion.

"I know." Sekhet's voice softened, just slightly. "But your death would destroy them both. Think, Ra. If you die, who protects Issah? Who guards your child against those who would harm it?"

The question pierced the fog in his mind. He had been so focused on the immediate need, on the daily act of giving blood, that he had lost sight of the larger war. Without him, Issah would be alone against Horus, against the Children of Darkness, against every threat that gathered in the shadows.

"Rest now," Sekhet commanded. "We'll find other ways."

They moved him to the bed, Issah's hands gentle as she arranged the cushions, wiped the sweat from his brow. Her belly pressed against him as she leaned close, a reminder of what they fought for.

"I'm sorry," she whispered.

"For what?"

"For letting you do this. For not seeing how much it was costing you."

He tried to smile, but his face felt like clay that wouldn't hold its shape. "I would give... everything. You know that."

"I know." She kissed his forehead, her lips warm against his cooling skin. "But I need you whole, Ra. I need you here, not sacrificed on an altar of good intentions."

Sleep pulled at him, a tide he couldn't resist. He fought it for a moment, not wanting to leave her, not wanting to surrender consciousness when danger still surrounded them. But his body had reached its limit, and it demanded rest.

"Thoth..." he managed before the darkness took him.

"We'll send for him again," Issah promised. "Rest now. I'll be here when you wake."

The dreams that came were strange, fractured things—memories of his godhood mixed with fears for the future, fragments of power that he could no longer touch. He dreamed of the solar barque, golden and glorious, carrying him across the sky while millions prayed his name. He dreamed of Issah's face, young and unmarked by the weight of leadership, from the first time he had seen her in the palace gardens. He dreamed of their child, faceless and perfect, reaching toward him with hands that glowed like stars.

When he woke, the sun was setting, painting the room in shades of amber and violet. Issah sat beside him, her hand resting on her belly, her eyes fixed on the middle distance as if seeing something he couldn't.

"How long?" he asked, his voice rough.

"Six hours." She turned to him, and he saw she had been crying. "You wouldn't wake up. I thought..."

"I'm here." He reached for her hand, found it cold and trembling. "I'm still here."

"Don't ever do that again." Her voice was fierce, a command rather than a request. "Don't give so much of yourself that there's nothing left. Promise me."

"I promise."

But even as he spoke, they both knew it was a lie. If the child needed blood and no other source could be found, he would give again. He would give until there was nothing left, because that was what love demanded.

Sekhet returned with broth and bread, watching with satisfaction as Ra consumed them. "Strength returns slowly," she said. "You'll need a week of rest, at minimum. More food, more sleep, no stress."

"We don't have a week," Ra said. "The infiltration of Set's temple—"

"Will wait," Issah cut in. "Or it will proceed without you. Kiya is capable. I'm capable. You are not."

The words stung more than he wanted to admit. He had been the sun itself, the ruler of gods and men. Now he was being told to rest while others fought his battles, to eat and sleep while his family faced danger.

But he couldn't argue with the weakness in his limbs, the gray fog that still clouded his thoughts. He was mortal now, bound by mortal limits, and those limits had finally caught up with him.

"Send for Thoth again," he said instead of arguing. "Use my name. Tell him... tell him that the last of the old sun asks for his help."

"He'll come," Issah said, though neither of them knew if it was true.

The night passed slowly, filled with small discomforts—the ache in his arm where the needle had entered, the strange lightness of his head, the vulnerability of lying helpless while the world continued without him. Issah dozed in a chair beside the bed, her hand on his chest, feeling his heartbeat.

In the darkest hours before dawn, Ra stared at the ceiling and confronted the truth he had been avoiding since his transformation: he was dying. Not immediately, not today, but slowly, inevitably, as all mortals died. Each blood donation accelerated the process, draining the divine residue that had sustained his mortal form. Sooner or later—and likely sooner, given the child's needs—his body would fail entirely.

The thought should have terrified him. For eight millennia, death had been an abstract concept, something that happened to others while he remained eternal. But instead of fear, he felt something else: acceptance. He had chosen mortality, chosen love, chosen a finite life over infinite power. The price was steep, but the alternative—eternity without Issah, without the child they had created together—was unthinkable.

He would die for them. It was as simple as that.

When morning came, he was stronger—still weak, still fragile, but no longer on the edge of collapse. He sat up in bed, accepted the food Issah brought him, and began to plan.

"I need to write letters," he said. "Instructions, in case..."

"Don't." Issah's voice was fierce. "Don't prepare for death while you're still alive."

"I'm preparing for all possibilities," he corrected gently. "That includes survival. But if I don't make it to see our child born, I want to leave something. Words they can read someday."

The fight went out of her. She brought him papyrus and ink, and watched as he wrote with a hand that still trembled slightly—letters to old allies, warnings to potential enemies, explanations for the child who might never know their father.

"When I'm done," he said, not looking up from his work, "we need to talk about the infiltration. Even if I can't go, I can help plan."

"Ra—"

"I won't be left out, Issah. I'm weak, not useless. My knowledge of the temple, of Set's defenses, of Shetu vulnerabilities—that's still valuable. Don't take that from me too."

She was quiet for a long moment, then nodded. "All right. We plan together. But when the time comes, you stay here. No arguments."

"No arguments," he agreed.

It was a lie, and they both knew it. But for now, in this moment of fragile peace, it was enough.

The weakness remained, a constant reminder of his new limitations. But as he wrote, as he planned, as he loved the woman beside him and the child growing within her, Ra found that mortality had its own kind of power.

Not the power to command the sun, but the power to choose. To sacrifice. To love enough to die.

And in that choice, he was more powerful than he had ever been as a god.
