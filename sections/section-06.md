Here's Section 6 - "Shadows" of Children of the Sun:

The moonlight cast long shadows through the abandoned temple's broken columns, stretching like grasping fingers across the cracked marble floor. Ra traced their movement, remembering when he once commanded such light himself. Now he waited in darkness like any other mortal, the weight of vulnerability pressing against his chest.

Issah's hand found his in the gloom. Her touch was warm, grounding, though he could feel the slight tremor in her fingers. The curve of her belly was more pronounced now, visible even beneath the loose folds of her dark cloak. Five months along, and their child grew stronger each day – as did the danger surrounding them.

"She's late," Issah whispered, her breath forming small clouds in the cold night air.

Ra squeezed her hand. "Kiya knows how to move unseen. She'll come."

As if summoned by his words, a shadow detached itself from the deeper darkness near the temple's rear entrance. Kiya emerged like a spirit, her movements liquid and silent. Her usually immaculate appearance was disheveled, dark circles shadowing her eyes.

"I wasn't followed," she said before either could ask. "But we don't have much time."

Ra felt Issah tense beside him. "What have you found?"

Kiya withdrew a leather portfolio from beneath her cloak, her hands trembling slightly as she opened it. "The Architect isn't working alone. This isn't just a radical faction – it's an organization with roots throughout the government. They call themselves the Children of Darkness."

The name sent a chill down Ra's spine. He remembered whispers of such a group from centuries past, but they had been dismissed as myth, a boogeyman story told to frighten young gods.

"How deep does it go?" Issah asked, one hand protectively cradling her belly.

Kiya spread several documents across a fallen column. In the wan moonlight, Ra could make out official seals, coded messages, and what appeared to be architectural drawings. "Deep enough to access restricted areas of the Celestial Palace. Deep enough to know Amenra's movements. Deep enough to plant false evidence and redirect investigations."

"A mole," Ra said, the word bitter on his tongue.

"More than one," Kiya corrected. "I've identified at least three separate channels of information flow, all at high levels. Someone on the Council itself may be compromised."

Issah drew in a sharp breath. "Set?"

"Possible, but unlikely. He's too obvious a suspect." Kiya's fingers traced the edge of a document. "This is personal for me. My mother... she investigated something similar thirty years ago. She disappeared before she could present her findings."

Ra studied Kiya's face, seeing for the first time the raw pain beneath her professional facade. "Why didn't you tell us this before?"

"Because I needed to be sure. The patterns match – the same methods, the same symbols. They've been building their power for decades, waiting for the right moment." She looked directly at Issah. "Your child represents everything they fear – the merging of divine and mortal, the potential for change."

Issah's free hand pressed more firmly against her stomach. "What do they want?"

"To maintain the old order. To keep the boundaries between mortal and divine absolute." Kiya's voice hardened. "They see your child as an abomination that will destroy everything they believe in."

Ra felt anger surge through him, hot and familiar. "Let them try-"

"No," Issah cut him off. "We can't fight them directly. Not when we don't know who to trust." She turned to Kiya. "What do you suggest?"

A gust of wind swept through the temple, scattering loose papers. Kiya quickly gathered them, her movements precise despite her exhaustion. "You need to disappear. Both of you. I have contacts in the mortal realm who can help, places even the Council can't reach."

"Run and hide?" Ra's voice was thick with disgust. "While they destroy everything we've built?"

"Ra." Issah's tone was gentle but firm. "Our child has to come first. We can't risk-"

"I won't live in fear," he interrupted. "I won't have our child born into shadows and secrets."

The silence that followed was heavy with unspoken tensions. Kiya watched them both, her expression unreadable. "Whatever you decide, it needs to be soon. They're moving faster now, growing bolder. The attack on Amenra was just the beginning."

Ra felt the familiar weight of responsibility settling across his shoulders. Once, he had carried the sun itself. Now he carried something far more precious and far more fragile.

"Three days," he said finally. "Give us three days to make arrangements. Then we'll decide."

Kiya nodded, gathering her documents. "I'll continue investigating, but be careful who you trust. The shadows have eyes, and they're watching."

After she had melted back into the darkness, Ra and Issah remained in the temple's ruins. The moon had shifted, casting new shadows across the ancient stones. Issah leaned against him, her warmth a counterpoint to the night's chill.

"We can't stay," she said softly. "You know that."

"I know." He wrapped an arm around her shoulders, feeling the slight tremor that ran through her body. "But I won't let them win. I won't let them turn our love into something shameful, something to hide."

Issah turned to face him, her eyes bright in the darkness. "Then we fight smart. We protect what matters most." She guided his hand to her belly, where their child moved beneath his palm. "Some battles are won by staying alive, by living well despite everything they throw at us."

Ra closed his eyes, feeling the gentle movement against his hand. In that moment, he understood with crystal clarity that his pride was a luxury he could no longer afford. The sun god in him might rage against hiding, against retreat, but the father in him knew better.

"Three days," he repeated. "We'll get what we need, contact who we must. Then we go."

They left the temple separately, as Kiya had instructed. Ra watched Issah disappear into the night, his heart heavy with love and fear. Above, the stars wheeled in their ancient patterns, indifferent to the struggles below. Somewhere in the darkness, enemies moved like shadows, drawing their nets tighter.

But shadows, Ra reminded himself, could not exist without light. And though he no longer carried the sun, its fire still burned in his blood, in the child growing beneath Issah's heart. Whatever came, they would face it together, children of both shadow and light.
