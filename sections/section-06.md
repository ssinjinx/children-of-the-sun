The moonlight cast long shadows through the abandoned temple's broken columns, stretching like grasping fingers across the cracked marble floor. Ra traced their movement with his eyes, remembering when he once commanded such light himself, when the darkness had bent to his will and retreated before his presence. Now he waited in darkness like any other mortal, the weight of vulnerability pressing against his chest with each breath he took.

The Temple of Khonsu had been magnificent once, dedicated to the moon god whose silver chariot still crossed the night sky. But the revolution had touched even sacred places, and now its halls stood empty, its treasures looted, its priests scattered to the winds. It was a fitting place for secrets, Ra thought—the house of the moon welcoming those who moved in shadow.

Issah's hand found his in the gloom. Her touch was warm, grounding, though he could feel the slight tremor in her fingers that spoke of exhaustion and fear. The curve of her belly was more pronounced now, visible even beneath the loose folds of her dark traveling cloak. Five months along, and their child grew stronger each day—as did the danger surrounding them.

"She's late," Issah whispered, her breath forming small clouds in the cold night air. The winter months had brought an unusual chill to Memphis, a cold that seemed to seep into the bones and refuse to leave.

Ra squeezed her hand. "Kiya knows how to move unseen. She'll come."

But he shared her anxiety. Every moment they spent outside their modest house was a moment of risk, every shadow potentially hiding a knife, every silence potentially concealing watchers. He had spent eight millennia being the thing others feared; learning to fear had been the hardest lesson of his mortality.

As if summoned by his words, a shadow detached itself from the deeper darkness near the temple's rear entrance. Kiya emerged like a spirit materializing from smoke, her movements liquid and silent. Her usually immaculate appearance was disheveled, her tight braids coming loose around her face, dark circles shadowing her sharp eyes. She carried herself like someone who hadn't slept in days.

"I wasn't followed," she said before either could ask, her voice rough from disuse. "But we don't have much time. They have patrols in the district now, checking the abandoned places."

Ra felt Issah tense beside him. "What have you found?"

Kiya withdrew a leather portfolio from beneath her cloak, her hands trembling slightly as she opened it. The leather was worn smooth from handling, stained with what looked like old blood at the corners. "The Architect isn't working alone. This isn't just a radical faction—it's an organization with roots throughout the government, the temples, even the military. They call themselves the Children of Darkness."

The name sent a chill down Ra's spine that had nothing to do with the winter air. He remembered whispers of such a group from centuries past, half-formed rumors that had surfaced during times of crisis and then disappeared again, dismissed as myth, a boogeyman story told to frighten young gods and keep them in line. He had never believed them real.

"How long have they been operating?" he asked, his voice barely above a whisper.

"Decades, perhaps longer." Kiya spread several documents across a fallen column that served as an impromptu table. In the wan moonlight, Ra could make out official seals, coded messages written in cipher, and what appeared to be architectural drawings of buildings he recognized—the palace, the council chambers, the very temple they stood in. "They're patient. They wait for moments of weakness, moments of transition. Like now."

"How deep does it go?" Issah asked, one hand protectively cradling her belly, unconsciously shielding their child from even the knowledge of danger.

Kiya's finger traced a line on one of the documents—a flow chart of names and connections that spiderwebbed across the page. "Deep enough to access restricted areas of the palace. Deep enough to know Amenra's exact schedule, his habits, his weaknesses. Deep enough to plant false evidence and redirect investigations before they can find the truth."

"A mole," Ra said, the word bitter on his tongue.

"More than one," Kiya corrected, her sharp eyes meeting his. "I've identified at least three separate channels of information flowing out of high-level meetings, all coordinated, all feeding into the same network. Someone on the Council itself may be compromised. Perhaps more than one someone."

Issah drew in a sharp breath, the sound loud in the temple's silence. "Set?"

"Possible, but unlikely. He's too obvious a suspect, too visible. The Children of Darkness prefer to work through cutouts, through people who have no idea who they're really serving." Kiya's fingers traced the edge of a document with particular care, as if it were fragile as eggshell. "But there's something else you need to know. This is personal for me. My mother... she investigated something similar thirty years ago. She disappeared before she could present her findings to the Council."

Ra studied Kiya's face in the moonlight, seeing for the first time the raw pain beneath her professional facade, the grief she kept locked away behind her sharp tongue and sharper eyes. "Why didn't you tell us this before?"

"Because I needed to be sure. The patterns match—the same methods, the same symbols, the same techniques for infiltration and elimination." Kiya's voice hardened, but Ra heard the crack in it, the emotion she was struggling to control. "She was close to exposing them when she vanished. Her body was never found, her notes destroyed. Officially, she was killed by bandits. But I know better. I've spent fifteen years learning how to investigate like she did, learning how to see what others miss."

"And now?" Issah asked gently.

"Now I've found proof. The same symbols she documented, the same communication patterns. They've been building their power for decades, waiting for the right moment to strike." Kiya looked directly at Issah, her gaze intense. "Your child represents everything they fear—the merging of divine and mortal, the potential for change that could upset the balance they've worked so hard to maintain."

Issah's free hand pressed more firmly against her stomach, as if she could physically shield their baby from the knowledge of what hunted it. "What do they want?"

"To maintain the old order. To keep the boundaries between mortal and divine absolute, unchanging, eternal." Kiya's voice hardened further, becoming the tool she used to distance herself from emotion. "They see your child as an abomination that will destroy everything they believe in. A symbol of corruption, of impurity. They will stop at nothing to prevent its birth."

Ra felt anger surge through him, hot and familiar, the divine rage he had thought he'd left behind when he surrendered his godhood. His hands curled into fists, nails digging into his palms. "Let them try—"

"No," Issah cut him off, her voice gentle but firm. She turned to face him, her eyes catching the moonlight. "We can't fight them directly. Not when we don't know who to trust, not when they could be anyone, anywhere."

"So we just hide?" Ra's voice was thick with frustration and the old pride that had been his downfall once before. "We let them dictate where we go, what we do, how we live?"

"We survive," Issah said. "That's not hiding, that's wisdom."

Kiya watched their exchange, her expression unreadable in the shadows. When she spoke, her voice was carefully neutral. "Whatever you decide, you need to understand the full scope of what you're facing. The Children of Darkness have infiltrated every level of society. The merchant who sells you bread could be their eyes. The servant who brings you water could be their ears. The friend you've known for years could be their voice."

"Then we trust no one," Ra said.

"You trust me," Kiya corrected. "And each other. That's all you have now."

She gathered her documents with practiced efficiency, returning them to the leather portfolio. "I have contacts in the lower districts, people who owe me favors, places even the Council doesn't know about. If you need to disappear, I can help. But you have to decide soon. They're accelerating their timeline. Whatever they have planned, it's coming faster than I anticipated."

"Three days," Ra said, the number feeling like a sentence. "Give us three days to make arrangements, to secure what we can, to prepare."

Kiya nodded, the movement sharp and economical. "I'll continue investigating, but be careful. The shadows have eyes, and they're watching more closely than you know." She paused at the edge of darkness, looking back at them. "For what it's worth... your mother would be proud of you, Issah. Fighting for a better world, even when it costs you everything. Mine would have said the same."

Then she was gone, melting into the shadows as if she had never been there at all.

Ra and Issah remained in the temple's ruins, the silence settling around them like dust. The moon had shifted higher in the sky, casting new shadows across the ancient stones, turning the broken columns into skeletons reaching toward the stars.

"We can't stay," Issah said softly, breaking the silence. "You know that."

"I know." He wrapped his arm around her shoulders, pulling her close against the night's chill. "But I won't let them win. I won't let them turn our love into something shameful, something to hide in shadows."

"Then we fight smart," Issah said, turning to face him. Her eyes were bright in the darkness, reflecting the moonlight. "We protect what matters most." She guided his hand to her belly, pressing it against the gentle curve where their child grew. "Some battles are won by staying alive, by living well despite everything they throw at us. We teach our child that love is stronger than hate, that hope outlasts fear."

Ra closed his eyes, feeling the subtle movements beneath his palm, the life that connected them to the future. In that moment, he understood with crystalline clarity that his pride was a luxury he could no longer afford, that the grand gestures of godhood had no place in the quiet heroism of parenthood. The sun god in him might rage against hiding, against retreat, against the indignity of running from shadows. But the father in him knew better.

"Three days," he repeated, the words becoming a vow. "We'll get what we need, contact who we must, do what we can to expose them. Then we go."

"Together," Issah added.

"Always."

They left the temple separately, as Kiya had instructed, taking different paths back to their house through the winding streets of Memphis. Ra walked with his hood pulled low, his senses alert for any sign of watchers, any hint of pursuit. But the streets were quiet, the city sleeping, the only sounds the distant barking of dogs and the whisper of wind through empty market stalls.

He reached their house first, lighting a single lamp against the darkness. When Issah arrived twenty minutes later, he pulled her inside and bolted the door, checking the windows, ensuring their small sanctuary was secure.

"We'll get through this," he said, more for himself than for her.

"We will," she agreed, and her certainty gave him strength. "We've faced worse."

He thought of all they had survived—the revolution, the transition, the loss of his godhood, the uncertainty of their future. She was right. They had faced worse. And they would face worse still, because the Children of Darkness would not rest until they had what they wanted.

But in the darkness of their small house, with Issah's warmth beside him and their child growing safe within her, Ra found something he had never possessed as a god: the quiet courage of a man fighting for his family, against odds that didn't matter because the stakes were everything.

The shadows were deep. But so was their love. And love, he was learning, was a light that no darkness could extinguish.
