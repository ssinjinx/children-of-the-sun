The birth came during the darkest night of the year, when even the stars seemed to hide their faces. Issah had known for hours that the time was near—the pains coming closer together, the pressure building until she could think of nothing else. But she had waited, enduring in silence, not wanting to call attention to their location.

They had moved three days earlier, fleeing Set's coup to a safe house that Kiya had prepared months before—a modest dwelling on the edge of the city, indistinguishable from hundreds of others, its very anonymity their best protection.

Now, as the first contractions became undeniable, there was no more waiting.

"Ra."

He was beside her in an instant, his face pale in the lamplight. "Is it—"

"Now." She gripped his hand as another wave washed over her, feeling her body prepare itself for what it had never done before, what no human body had ever done. "Send for the midwife."

But the midwife was hours away, and the baby would not wait. Ra sent for her anyway, and for Sekhet—despite everything, they still needed a healer who understood Shetu physiology—but prepared to deliver the child himself if necessary.

"I've seen it done," he said, more to reassure himself than her. "In my time as a god, I watched over countless births. I know what to do."

"You were a god then," Issah gasped, the pain cresting higher now. "Now you're—"

"Yours." He knelt beside her, taking her hand in both of his. "I'm yours, Issah. And I'm here. That's what matters."

The hours that followed were the longest of Ra's existence, longer than any century of godhood, any millennium of rule. He held Issah's hand through each contraction, wiped the sweat from her brow, whispered encouragement when her strength flagged and her courage wavered.

"I can't," she sobbed at one point, her body shaking with exhaustion. "I can't do this."

"You can." He pressed his forehead to hers, sharing his breath, his strength, his love. "You have done impossible things before, my queen. You freed a people. You loved a god. You can do this."

And she did. Through the night and into the gray morning, she pushed, she strained, she gave everything she had to bring their child into the world.

When the baby finally emerged, silent and still, Ra's heart stopped.

Then it cried—a sound like music, like thunder, like the first word of creation. And Ra's heart started again, beating now for three instead of two.

"A girl," he whispered, lifting the child with hands that trembled. She was perfect, tiny, her skin a golden shade between her mother's brown and the pale luminosity of the Shetu. Her eyes, when they opened, were the color of amber—neither human nor divine but something entirely new.

"Let me see her." Issah's voice was hoarse, barely audible, but her arms reached out with unerring accuracy.

Ra placed the child in her mother's arms, and Issah's face transformed. All the pain, all the fear, all the exhaustion fell away, replaced by a love so fierce it seemed to illuminate the room.

"Hello, little one," she whispered, tracing the baby's cheek with a shaking finger. "Welcome to the world."

The midwife arrived an hour later, clucking over the afterbirth, checking mother and child, pronouncing them both healthy despite the unusual circumstances. Sekhet came too, escorted under guard, her face unreadable as she examined the child she had once sought to destroy.

"She is... remarkable," the traitor-healer said finally, her clinical tone barely masking her wonder. "Neither fully Shetu nor fully human. Something else entirely. The blood transfusions worked—her physiology is stable, self-sustaining. She won't need regular feeding to survive."

"Will she have powers?" Ra asked.

"I don't know. No one knows." Sekhet met his eyes, and for the first time, he saw something other than hatred there—curiosity, perhaps, or the dawning recognition of what she had almost destroyed. "She is unprecedented. The first of her kind."

"The first of many," Issah said, her voice fierce despite her weakness. "Whether you like it or not, Sekhet. The future doesn't care about your prejudices."

The healer bowed her head, accepting the rebuke. "I will not apologize for my beliefs, my queen. But I will acknowledge that I may have been... mistaken about the danger this child represents."

"Mistaken?" Ra laughed, a sound of genuine amazement. "You tried to kill my family, and now you're 'mistaken'?"

"I am a healer. I swore an oath to preserve life." Sekhet's voice was quiet, almost broken. "When I saw this child, saw that she truly lives, truly breathes, truly exists... I realized that my definition of life had been too narrow. She is alive. She is real. And she deserves the chance to become whatever she will become."

"Words," Ra said. "Pretty words after the fact."

"All I have to offer." Sekhet turned to leave, then paused at the door. "She will need protection. There are those who will see her existence as a threat even greater than her conception. The Children of Darkness have not given up. Set has not given up. The danger is only beginning."

"We know," Issah said, her arms tightening around her daughter. "We'll be ready."

When they were alone again, the three of them in the small safe house, Ra sat beside Issah and truly looked at his child for the first time. She was sleeping now, her tiny chest rising and falling with the rhythm of new life, her fingers curled into fists as if ready to fight for her place in the world.

"What should we name her?" he asked.

Issah was quiet for a moment, considering. "In the old stories, the first light of dawn was called Aten. The disk of the sun, the source of all life."

"Aten." Ra tasted the name, feeling its weight. "It suits her. The child of the sun, born from love between mortal and divine."

"Aten," Issah agreed, and the baby stirred at the sound of her name, her amber eyes opening to fix on her mother's face.

In that moment, looking at his wife and child, Ra felt something shift inside him—a settling, a peace, a knowledge that whatever came next, they had already won the most important battle. They had created life where none should exist. They had brought hope into a world that desperately needed it.

Set's coup, the Children of Darkness, the fractured government—all of it faded into the background before this simple truth: they were a family, and they were together, and that was enough.

For now, at least, it was enough.
