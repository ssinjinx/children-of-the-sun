The trail of gold led deeper than Kiya had expected, winding through the merchant houses of Memphis like a serpent through grass. She had spent two days following Set's lead, watching Khufu's movements, tracing the flow of funds from his accounts to destinations that dissolved into shadow.

The breakthrough came on the third day, in a counting house near the river district.

Kiya sat across from a nervous scribe named Ptah-hotep, a man whose fingers trembled as he traced entries in a ledger that predated the revolution. The room smelled of old papyrus and fear-sweat, the only sound the scratching of his reed pen and the distant calls of boatmen on the Nile.

"These transfers," Kiya said, tapping the page with her dagger's tip. "They started when?"

"Three years ago, mistress. Small amounts at first, then larger sums as the... the movement grew."

"And the recipient?"

Ptah-hotep swallowed hard, his eyes darting to the shuttered window as if expecting rescue. "The accounts are held under a false name. But the seals, the authentication marks... they match the treasury of the House of the Falcon."

Kiya felt a cold certainty settle in her gut. Horus. The sun god who had replaced Ra, who wore the mantle of divine authority with the impatience of youth. If he was funding the Children of Darkness...

"Continue," she said, her voice steady despite the turmoil within.

"The money flows through three intermediaries before reaching its final destination. I've traced two of them—both dead now, killed in accidents that were not accidents. The third..." He paused, his pen hovering over the page. "The third is still alive. A merchant named Besenmut who deals in copper from Sinai."

"Where can I find him?"

"His warehouse. Near the quays. But mistress..." Ptah-hotep looked up at her, and she saw genuine fear in his eyes. "He is protected. Not by guards, but by something else. I've heard whispers that he keeps Shetu enforcers, blood-bound and deadly."

Kiya smiled, and it was not a pleasant expression. "I've dealt with Shetu before."

She left the counting house through the back entrance, melting into the crowd that thronged the riverside market. The sun was high, turning the Nile into a ribbon of beaten bronze, and she used the brightness as cover—who would expect an assassin in broad daylight?

The warehouse district was a maze of mud-brick buildings, their walls sweating with the heat, their interiors dark as tombs. Kiya moved with practiced silence, her leather boots making no sound on the packed earth. She found Besenmut's warehouse by following the smell of hot metal, the acrid tang of copper refining.

The entrance was guarded by two men who were clearly not human. Their skin was too pale, their movements too fluid, their eyes too sharp. Shetu, or close enough to make no difference. Blood-bound, Ptah-hotep had said. Servants of whoever held their leash.

Kiya didn't try to sneak past them. Instead, she approached openly, her investigator's badge displayed prominently. "I have questions for Besenmut," she said. "Official business of the Transitional Council."

The taller of the two guards studied her with eyes like polished obsidian. "The merchant is not receiving visitors."

"I wasn't asking."

The violence that followed was brief and brutal. Kiya had trained for fifteen years to fight opponents stronger and faster than herself. She didn't try to match them strength for strength—she used their momentum against them, striking pressure points, exploiting the vulnerabilities that all flesh shared regardless of its origin.

When it was over, one guard lay unconscious against the wall, the other cradling a broken arm and staring at her with something approaching respect.

"Tell Besenmut," she said, stepping over the fallen guard, "that the next visitor might not be as polite."

The warehouse interior was a cathedral of shadows, its high windows casting bars of light across stacks of copper ingots. A man sat at a desk in the center, surrounded by ledgers and scales, his face hidden by the brim of an expensive hat.

"I wondered when someone would come," he said, not looking up from his accounts. "I expected Ra himself, or perhaps that human queen of his. Not a lone investigator."

"I'm thorough," Kiya said, moving to stand across from him. "And I'm patient. Two qualities your employers seem to lack."

Besenmut finally looked up, revealing a face that had once been handsome before greed carved its lines. "The Children of Darkness are not my employers, investigator. They are my clients. There's a difference."

"Not to me. Where does the money go after it leaves your accounts?"

"I don't know. That's the point of the system—compartmentalization, deniability. I receive funds from Khufu's intermediaries, transfer them to other accounts, take my percentage. What happens after that is not my concern."

"Make it your concern."

Besenmut's smile was oily, confident in his protection. "Or what? You'll arrest me? I have contracts with half the council, investigator. I donate to temples, support charities, employ hundreds. I'm a pillar of the community."

"Pillars can be toppled."

"And houses fall when they do." He leaned forward, his eyes narrowing. "Let me give you some advice, freely offered. Whatever you're investigating, stop. The forces at play here are older than your council, older than your revolution, older than the Shetu themselves. You cannot win against them."

"I don't need to win," Kiya said. "I just need to find the truth."

"The truth?" Besenmut laughed, a harsh sound. "The truth is that your precious transitional government is a fiction, a temporary arrangement while the real powers decide how to reshape the world. The truth is that your queen's unborn child is an abomination that threatens everything, and there are those who will do whatever is necessary to prevent its birth. The truth is that you're already dead, investigator—you just haven't fallen down yet."

Kiya moved faster than he could react, her dagger at his throat before he could summon his guards. "And the truth," she whispered, "is that I don't care about any of that. I care about justice for my mother. I care about stopping killers who hide behind ancient names and false ideals. And I care very much about making people like you tell me what I want to know."

She pressed the blade just hard enough to draw a thin line of blood. "The final destination of the funds. Tell me."

"The temple," he gasped, his confidence finally cracking. "The old temple of Set, in the red lands. That's where the money goes. That's where they meet."

"Who leads them?"

"I don't know! I swear, I never met anyone above the level of courier. They use masks, code names, intermediaries. I know only what I need to know to do my job."

Kiya held the blade a moment longer, reading the truth in his terrified eyes. Then she released him, stepping back into the shadows. "If you're lying..."

"I'm not! Please, just go. If they find out I talked..."

"Then you'd better hope I succeed."

She left him there, shaking and sweating, and made her way back through the warehouse district with the setting sun at her back. The information was valuable—the temple of Set, abandoned since the revolution, would be the perfect meeting place for a conspiracy. But it wasn't enough. She needed names, faces, the identities of those who commanded rather than served.

And she needed to know if Set himself was involved, or if his brother had betrayed him as thoroughly as everyone else.

The walk back to the city took until full dark, the stars emerging overhead in their ancient patterns. Kiya used the time to think, to plan, to prepare for what came next. If the Children of Darkness truly met at Set's old temple, then the next meeting would be where she made her move.

But she couldn't do it alone. She needed help—Ra's knowledge of divine places, Issah's authority to command resources, perhaps even Set's insider knowledge if he proved trustworthy.

The game was larger than she had realized, the stakes higher than any she had played for. But Kiya had spent her entire adult life preparing for this moment, training for this hunt. Whatever the cost, she would see it through.

Her mother deserved that much. Amenra deserved that much. And the child growing in Issah's womb deserved a chance to live in a world free from shadow.

She found Ra and Issah at their house, waiting with the tense patience of those who had learned that news could be either salvation or destruction. Kiya gave them what she had learned—the money trail, the temple, the compartmentalized structure that protected the leaders from exposure.

"Set's temple," Ra said, his face unreadable. "If he's playing us, this confirms it. If he's telling the truth..."

"Then someone else has taken over his old stronghold," Issah finished. "Someone with the power to command both Shetu and human servants."

"Horus," Kiya said. "The money comes from his treasury. The timing aligns with his rise to power. And he has the most to gain from eliminating your child—removing a potential rival before it can challenge his authority."

"But we still don't have proof," Ra said. "Suspicion isn't enough to move against a god."

"Then we get proof," Kiya said. "The temple will have records, witnesses, something we can use. We just need to get inside."

"And survive," Issah added quietly.

"That too."

They planned through the night, the three of them huddled over maps and diagrams, preparing for an infiltration that could end with all their deaths. But as the sky began to lighten and the first birds called from the rooftops, Kiya felt something she hadn't felt in years: hope.

The trail was warm. The prey was in sight. And soon, very soon, the hunt would end.

One way or another.
