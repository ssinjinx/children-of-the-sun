Thoth came to them three days after the birth, his ibis head bowed with a weight that had nothing to do with physical burden. He found them in the safe house, surrounded by Kiya's guards, the new family huddled together in the small room that had become their sanctuary.

"The signal has changed," he said without preamble. "It's no longer passive. It's... demanding."

Ra looked up from where he sat beside Issah, their daughter sleeping in her arms. "Demanding how?"

"It's become directional. Focused." Thoth produced his instruments—crystalline devices that hummed with otherworldly energy. "It's targeting this location, Ra. It's targeting your child."

"What does that mean?"

"It means the signal knows. Somehow, across distances I cannot measure, through means I cannot understand—it knows that Aten exists. And it wants her."

Issah's arms tightened around her daughter. "Wants her for what?"

"I believe..." Thoth hesitated, his avian features struggling with an emotion that might have been awe or might have been fear. "I believe the signal is a beacon. A rescue signal, sent millennia ago when the Shetu first arrived here. It's been waiting all this time for a receiver capable of answering. And now, finally, it has found one."

"Aten is not a radio," Ra said sharply. "She's a baby."

"She's a bridge." Thoth's voice was gentle but firm. "The signal requires a specific type of biology to receive and amplify it. Something that exists in both realms—the physical and the... other. Your daughter has that biology, Ra. She could complete the circuit. She could bring the signal home."

"At what cost?"

Thoth was silent for a long moment, his instruments humming quietly in the background. "I don't know. The energy required would be... significant. It might drain her, temporarily or permanently. It might change her, alter her development. Or it might simply use her as a conduit, leaving her unharmed."

"Or it might kill her," Issah said, her voice flat.

"That too is possible."

Ra stood, moving to the window where the afternoon sun painted the room in gold. His daughter's namesake, he thought bitterly. The sun's light, warming them even as darkness gathered.

"You want us to risk our child's life," he said, not turning. "To satisfy your curiosity, to answer questions that have waited millennia."

"I want us to consider the possibility," Thoth corrected. "The signal represents the greatest mystery in existence—where we came from, how we got here, whether we can ever return. Your daughter might be the only chance we have to solve that mystery."

"And if solving it destroys her?"

"Then we will have learned something important about the cost of knowledge." Thoth's voice carried no trace of irony—only the honest conviction of someone who had dedicated his existence to understanding. "Everything has a price, Ra. The question is whether the answer is worth the asking."

Issah rose from the bed, placing Aten in her cradle before facing the god of wisdom. "When she is old enough to choose for herself," she said, her voice carrying the authority of motherhood, "we will ask her. And whatever she decides, we will support. But until then, she is not a tool for your research. She is our daughter. And we will protect her from anyone who would use her, no matter how noble their intentions."

Thoth bowed, accepting the boundary she had drawn. "I understand. And I will respect your decision, for now. But the signal will not wait forever. It's growing stronger, more insistent. Whatever is sending it knows that a receiver exists, and it will keep trying to establish contact."

"Then we'll deal with that when it becomes a problem," Ra said. "For now, we have more immediate concerns. Set's coup. The Children of Darkness. Surviving long enough to raise our daughter in peace."

"Peace may not be possible," Thoth observed. "The signal is not the only force gathering around your child. I have detected others—energies I don't recognize, presences that don't fit any category in my knowledge. Something is coming, Ra. Something larger than Set, larger than the Children of Darkness, larger than anything we have faced before."

"Then we'll face it together," Ra said. "As a family."

Thoth gathered his instruments, preparing to leave. At the door, he paused. "For what it's worth... I am glad she exists. Your daughter. Whatever she becomes, whoever she chooses to be—her existence is a miracle. And I'm honored to witness it."

"Thank you, old friend." Ra's voice was softer now, the anger fading. "For everything."

When Thoth was gone, Ra and Issah sat together beside Aten's cradle, watching their daughter sleep. The choice Thoth had offered sat heavy between them—the possibility of answers against the certainty of risk.

"What do we do?" Issah asked finally.

"We wait," Ra said. "We watch. We prepare. And when the time comes—if the time comes—we trust our daughter to make her own choice."

"She's so small." Issah's finger traced the air above Aten's face, not quite touching. "So fragile. And already the world demands so much from her."

"She's stronger than she looks." Ra placed his hand on Issah's shoulder, feeling her warmth, her life. "She has your courage, your determination. And she has us. Whatever comes, we'll face it together."

"Together," Issah agreed.

Outside, the sun set on another day, and somewhere in the darkness between stars, a signal pulsed—waiting, hoping, demanding. The choice would come, sooner or later. But for now, in this small room, there was only love, only family, only the fragile peace of the present moment.

And that, for now, was enough.
