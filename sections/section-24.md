The convergence happened not at the Solar Barque but in the courtyard of a ruined house, where a former god gave his life so that his daughter might become what she was born to be. The light from the stars met the light from Aten, and for a moment, all the factions—Set's hardliners, the Children of Darkness, the resistance fighters, the curious bystanders—stood together in wonder.

Ra felt himself dissolving, not into death but into something else. His essence, his ka, the divine spark that had survived even his surrender of godhood—it was flowing into Aten, becoming part of the bridge, the connection, the link between worlds.

"No!" Set's voice, distant and desperate. "There must be another way!"

"There is no other way," the voice of the Architects replied. "Only love. Only sacrifice. Only the willingness to give everything for something greater than oneself."

Ra felt Issah's hands on him, holding him, trying to keep him anchored to the world. He wanted to tell her to let go, to accept, to understand that this was not an ending but a transformation. But he had no voice anymore, no form, only love and light and the infinite moment of choice.

Aten glowed brighter, her small body containing the energy of a dying star, a mortal life, a father's love. She was the bridge now, fully and completely, the connection between Earth and the world of the Shetu's origin, between the present and the ancient past, between what was and what could be.

"It's working," Thoth breathed, his instruments showing readings that had no precedent. "The connection is stabilizing. The bridge is forming."

"Ra," Issah whispered, her tears falling on his fading form. "Don't leave me. Please."

"Never," he tried to say, though he wasn't sure if the words reached her. "I'm always with you. In her. In the bridge. In the light."

The last thing he saw was his daughter's face, her amber eyes wide with understanding beyond her years, her small hand reaching toward him even as he reached toward her. Their fingers touched, and in that touch, the circuit completed.

The light exploded outward, enveloping everything, everyone, washing over Memphis like a tide of gold. And when it faded, when the world could see again, Ra was gone.

But the bridge remained.

Aten stood alone for a moment, glowing softly, then turned to her mother and held out her arms. Issah gathered her close, feeling the warmth that was not quite human, not quite divine, but something new and precious and alive.

"Papa," Aten said, her first word in the new world. "Papa is here."

And Issah felt it too—the presence that was Ra, not gone but transformed, part of the bridge now, part of the connection, part of the love that would forever bind the worlds together.

Set fell back, his coup forgotten, his ambition meaningless in the face of what had been accomplished. "What have you done?" he whispered.

"What was necessary," Meritamen replied, her ancient voice steady. "What love demanded."

The crowd that had gathered—Shetu and human, friend and enemy, believer and skeptic—stood in silence, witnessing the birth of a new age. The bridge was open. The worlds were connected. And a child who was neither human nor divine but both and neither stood at the center of it all, her father's love her foundation, her mother's strength her guide.

The convergence was complete.

And the future, at last, could begin.
