The alliances formed slowly, like ice on a winter pond—fragile, dangerous, necessary. Kiya worked her networks, calling in favors from years of service, building bridges between groups that had never trusted each other before.

There were those who stood with Issah and Ra without hesitation—Meritamen, who believed in the dream of coexistence; Khenmu, who had seen Issah's leadership save lives; a handful of human delegates who understood that Set's path led only to war. And there were Shetu who had rejected the hardliners' fear, who saw in Aten not a threat but a promise.

But others wavered, caught between old loyalties and new realities. Horus maintained his neutrality, offering neither help nor hindrance, his divine power withheld like a sword kept sheathed. Some called him coward; others saw wisdom in his restraint.

Set, for his part, offered simple clarity: join him or be destroyed. His forces grew daily, swallowing districts, converting the fearful and the angry. The city was dividing, street by street, household by household, into factions that would soon be armies.

"We need Thoth," Issah said one evening, as they reviewed the maps showing Set's territory expanding like a stain. "We need his wisdom, his knowledge. And we need him to choose a side."

"Thoth doesn't choose sides," Ra replied. "He chooses knowledge. And right now, Set is offering him something we can't—access to the old archives, the forbidden texts, the secrets that have been locked away for centuries."

"Then we offer him something better."

"What?"

Issah looked at her daughter, sleeping peacefully despite the chaos surrounding her. "The future. A chance to see what his theories actually mean in practice. If Thoth wants to understand hybrid biology, what better laboratory than Aten herself?"

"You would let him experiment on her?"

"I would let him observe. Learn. Grow." Issah's voice was firm. "The signal wants to use her as a conduit. Set wants to destroy her. The Children of Darkness want to erase her existence entirely. Thoth wants to understand her. Of all of them, his desire is the least destructive. And if we can redirect it..."

"Turn the seeker of knowledge into an ally," Ra finished. "It's risky."

"Everything is risky now." Issah took his hand. "But we need him, Ra. We need every ally we can get."

They sent for Thoth that night, and the god of wisdom came, his instruments packed away, his curiosity evident in every line of his avian features.

"You wish to propose an alliance," he said, not waiting for preamble.

"We wish to propose a partnership," Issah corrected. "You want to study hybrid biology. We have the only living example. In exchange for your support against Set and the Children of Darkness, we offer you access—limited, supervised, but real—to Aten's development."

Thoth's eyes gleamed. "You would trust me with her? After I suggested using her for the signal?"

"We don't trust you," Ra said honestly. "But we need you. And we believe that your curiosity, properly directed, can serve us all."

"A dangerous gamble."

"The only kind left."

Thoth was silent for a long moment, his head tilted in thought. Then, slowly, he nodded. "Very well. I will stand with you. Not because I believe in your cause—though I find I am growing to—but because the alternative is ignorance, and I have never been able to abide that."

"Welcome to the resistance," Kiya said dryly from her place by the door.

With Thoth came others—scholars and seekers who valued knowledge over dogma, who saw in Aten a chance to understand rather than to fear. They brought with them resources: texts that could counter Set's propaganda, techniques for defending against Shetu power, strategies refined through centuries of study.

But they also brought complications. Not all of Thoth's followers agreed with his choice; some defected to Set, taking knowledge with them. The alliance was powerful but fragile, held together by common interest rather than common belief.

"It will hold long enough," Thoth predicted, when Ra expressed his concerns. "Long enough for what comes next."

"What comes next?"

The god of wisdom smiled, mysterious and knowing. "The convergence. Everything we've been building toward. The moment when all sides must choose, finally and forever, what kind of world they want to live in."

He wouldn't say more, but his words haunted Ra's dreams that night. The convergence. The endgame. The moment when the child of sun and mortal would become not just a symbol but a weapon, not just a hope but a force that would reshape reality itself.

And when that moment came, Ra knew, the choices they made would echo through eternity.

He held his daughter that night, feeling her warmth, her life, her infinite potential. She stirred in her sleep, her amber eyes opening briefly to fix on his face, and he felt the love surge through him like a physical force.

Whatever came, he would protect her. Whatever the cost, he would pay it. That was the choice he had made, the side he had chosen, the only side that mattered.

Family.
