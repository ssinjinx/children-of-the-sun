Ra woke to pain.

It bloomed in his joints like desert flowers after rain—sudden, insistent, and somehow both familiar and foreign even after two years. His shoulder ached where it pressed against the reed mat, and his hip protested when he shifted his weight. The sensations still startled him each morning, these small betrayals of flesh he'd never known in eight millennia of godhood.

Beside him, Issah slept deeply, her breath steady as the Nile's flow. Her dark hair spilled across the pillow, and in the pre-dawn light filtering through the window, he could see the gentle rise and fall of her chest. He watched her, memorizing every detail as he did each morning: the curve of her cheek, the slight part of her lips, the small scar above her eyebrow from a childhood fall. She looked younger in sleep, though the past two years had etched fine lines around her eyes—the weight of leadership marking her face as surely as time now marked his.

Ra eased himself up, careful not to wake her. His bones creaked—another strange symphony he was still learning to interpret. Each morning brought new discoveries in this mortal form: a fresh ache, an unfamiliar twinge, the slow erosion of what he had once been. The ka particles that had sustained him for millennia were gone, sacrificed for love of the woman sleeping beside him. He didn't regret the choice, but some mornings the price felt heavier than others.

The eastern sky was beginning to pale. Once, he would have felt the approach of dawn in his very essence, his consciousness extending to the great ship that had been his home and throne for eight thousand years. Now he experienced sunrise as humans did: a gradual brightening, a slow warmth, the world revealing itself one shadow at a time.

He stood, unsteady for the first few moments as his blood adjusted to the change in position. Another mortal peculiarity—these moments of dizziness, this need to wait for his body to catch up with his intentions. His feet were cold against the packed earth floor, and he could feel every rough spot, every small stone. Without the ka particles constantly repairing his cells, his skin had grown softer, more sensitive. The world pressed against him now in ways it never had before.

Walking to the window, Ra looked out over the sleeping city. Memphis sprawled below their modest house, its white walls catching the first hints of dawn. No longer did he dwell in the great palace that had been his home for millennia. That building stood empty now, a monument to what had been. The new council chambers where Issah led the provisional government were purposefully humble—a statement about the changes they were trying to create.

From his vantage point, Ra could see the great palace where he had once been worshipped, its golden caps now dulled by distance and dawn shadow. The temple complex stretched beneath it like a sleeping cat, massive and self-contained. Two years ago, he would have been aboard his solar barque, preparing for the day's journey across the sky. The ship had been an extension of himself, a living thing of gold and light that responded to his smallest thought. Now it rested in the hands of Horus, who had taken up the mantle of sun god with the eager determination of youth.

Ra pressed his palm against the sun-warmed mud brick of the window frame. The rough texture grounded him in the present, though his mind still drifted to memories of smooth golden walls and the constant hum of ka energy that had surrounded him for millennia. He had given up more than just immortality when he chose to become human. He had surrendered an entire way of being—the ability to exist in multiple places at once, to feel the prayers of millions brush against his consciousness like wind through wheat, to know with absolute certainty his place in the great machinery of existence.

Now his thoughts stayed trapped inside his skull, his awareness limited to what his mortal senses could tell him. The loss still ached, a phantom limb he sometimes reached for in moments of distraction. But the trade had been fair: godhood for love, certainty for possibility. Still, on mornings like this, when his joints protested and his muscles reminded him of their new limitations, doubt crept in like a desert wind.

Death waited for him now, no longer a distant concept but an approaching reality. He could feel it in the way his body changed, in the small deteriorations that marked each passing month. How many years did he have? Twenty? Thirty? A mere eyeblink compared to the millennia he had already lived. Would his ka survive death as human kas did? Would he meet Osiris as a supplicant rather than an equal? These questions haunted him in the quiet hours before dawn, when the barrier between what was and what might be grew thin.

Behind him, the reed mat rustled as Issah stirred. He turned to watch her wake, a process that still fascinated him with its small intimacies. She stretched like a cat, her arms reaching above her head, then blinked slowly in the growing light. When her eyes found him at the window, she smiled—that same smile that had first made him question everything he had been.

"You're up early," she said, her voice still rough with sleep. "Bad dreams?"

"No dreams," he replied, moving back to sit on the edge of their sleeping mat. "Just thoughts."

She sat up, the linen sheet pooling around her waist, and reached for his hand. Her fingers were warm against his, and he marveled again at how such a simple touch could anchor him so completely to the present moment. "Heavy thoughts, by the look of you."

"The past weighs more some mornings than others." He brought her hand to his lips, kissing her knuckles. "But the present has its compensations."

Issah's free hand came up to cup his cheek, her thumb tracing the line of his jaw where silver had begun to thread through his dark beard. "You're brooding again about time, aren't you?"

"It moves differently now," he admitted. "Faster. More... urgent."

"That's because you're finally living in it instead of watching it pass." She leaned forward to kiss him, morning breath and all—another human reality he had learned to accept, even appreciate for its honesty. "Now come help me with breakfast. The market will be busy today, and I need to get there before the best figs are gone."

They rose together, performing the small ritual of dressing that had become familiar over the past two years. Ra pulled on a simple linen shendyt, the kind worn by scribes and merchants rather than the elaborately pleated royal garment he had once favored. Issah donned a long dress, its white fabric brightened by the red sash she tied at her waist. They moved around each other with practiced ease, sharing the narrow space of their bedroom as they prepared for the day.

In the main room, Ra kindled the cooking fire while Issah ground grain for bread. The domestic sounds—stone against stone, the crack and pop of burning wood—had become as much a part of his daily rhythm as the rising of the sun had once been. He fetched water from the clay jar in the corner, poured some into a basin for washing, and began the morning ablutions that were now necessary rather than ceremonial.

The cool water on his face helped clear the last cobwebs of sleep from his mind. He ran wet fingers through his hair, now streaked with gray at the temples, and watched his reflection waver in the basin. The face that looked back at him was both familiar and strange—still his features, but softened by humanity, marked by time in ways his divine form had never been.

They ate their morning meal of bread, dates, and watered wine sitting cross-legged on reed mats, talking of ordinary things: the price of grain in the market, the new well being dug near the temple, the upcoming festival of Opet. Ra found himself increasingly drawn into these mundane concerns, his ancient perspective gradually giving way to the immediate needs and rhythms of mortal life.

"Will you come to the market with me?" Issah asked as she gathered the empty dishes.

Ra shook his head. "I have promised to help Amenemhat with his accounts today. The old man's eyes are failing, and he trusts few others with his records."

It still amused him sometimes, how he had fallen into the role of scribe and accountant. His knowledge of hieroglyphs was unparalleled, of course, and numbers had always been sacred to him. Now he used these divine skills to help merchants balance their books and priests record their inventories. It was honest work, far removed from the cosmic responsibilities he had once held, but it gave him purpose and a place in the human world he had chosen to join.

He gathered his writing materials: sheets of papyrus, reed pens, cakes of black and red ink. Each item went into the leather satchel Issah had made for him, the strap worn smooth from daily use. She handed him a small packet of bread and dried fruit for his midday meal, and he felt the now-familiar surge of love for her thoughtfulness.

"Try not to let Amenemhat talk your ear off about the old days," she said, straightening his collar. "You know how he gets once he starts reminiscing about his youth."

Ra smiled, thinking of the elderly merchant's endless stories about Memphis in its glory days—days that, to Ra, were barely yesterday. "I'll do my best, though some of his tales are worth hearing."

"Just be home before sunset," Issah said, rising on her toes to kiss him goodbye. "I'm making your favorite tonight—duck with pomegranates."

He held her close for a moment, breathing in the scent of her hair—lotus oil and sleep-warmth. Then he stepped back, shouldered his satchel, and moved toward the door. The morning sun had fully risen now, painting the streets gold and shadow. As he stepped out into the growing heat of the day, Ra felt the familiar mix of loss and gratitude that colored all his mornings now: loss for what he had been, gratitude for what he had become.

The streets of Memphis were already busy with vendors setting up their stalls, children running errands, workers heading to the fields and workshops. Ra moved among them, no longer the distant god they had once prayed to, but simply another man beginning his day's work. He touched the smooth stone scarab he wore at his throat—a reminder of who he had been, who he was becoming—and turned his steps toward the merchant's quarter, where Amenemhat's house waited with its stacks of accounts and its windows open to the morning light.
