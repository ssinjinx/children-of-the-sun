The first wave of Set's soldiers died on Ra's blade, their divine strength no match for his desperation. He fought with the fury of a father protecting his child, the determination of a man with nothing left to lose. Each strike was precise, each defense calculated, his mortal body pushed beyond its limits by adrenaline and love.

But they kept coming. Set had sent his best—Shetu warriors who had trained for centuries, who knew every form of combat, every strategy of war. They came through the windows, through the roof, through walls that should have been solid.

Ra fell back, room by room, buying seconds with blood. His own blood, mostly—the cuts on his arms, his chest, his face, stinging with the salt of sweat and the fire of exertion. He was slowing, weakening, his mortal form reaching its limits.

"Surrender," a voice called from beyond the shattered doorway. Set himself, finally showing his face. "Surrender, brother, and I'll let them live. Issah, the child—I'll give them sanctuary."

"Liar," Ra gasped, leaning against the wall for support.

"Pragmatist." Set stepped into the room, his divine form glowing with power that Ra could no longer match. "This doesn't have to end in death, Ra. Step aside. Let me take the child. I'll raise her as my own, teach her what she needs to know. She'll be safe, powerful, respected."

"She'll be a prisoner."

"She'll be alive. Which is more than I can promise if you continue to resist."

Ra pushed himself upright, raising his sword one final time. "No."

Set sighed, genuine regret in his eyes. "Then I'm sorry, brother. Truly."

The blast of divine power should have killed him instantly. It threw him through the wall, through the next room, through the outer wall of the house itself. He landed in the courtyard, broken and burning, his body screaming with pain that no mortal should have survived.

But he did survive. Barely. Consciousness flickering, vision blurred, he saw Set approach, saw the regret on his brother's face, saw the hand raised to finish what the blast had started.

Then the sky opened.

Not metaphorically. Literally. The clouds parted, the darkness receding before a light that was not sunlight, not starlight, but something else entirely. The signal, Thoth had called it. The beacon from beyond.

And it was coming down.

A column of light struck the courtyard, not burning but transforming, not destroying but changing. Set stumbled back, shielding his eyes, his divine power suddenly insignificant before what descended from the heavens.

Ra felt it too—the light touching him, healing him, filling him with strength he hadn't possessed since surrendering his godhood. But it wasn't for him. It was passing through him, seeking something else, someone else.

Aten.

From the cellar entrance, Issah emerged, their daughter in her arms. The child was glowing, her amber eyes wide with wonder, reaching toward the light with tiny hands that seemed to grasp at infinity.

"No!" Set screamed, realizing too late what was happening. "Stop her! Stop the signal!"

But it was too late. The connection was made. The bridge was formed. And the world would never be the same.
