The choices came slowly at first, then in a flood. Shetu who had lived for millennia in Egypt stepped forward to claim their heritage, to return to the world they had lost. Others—those who had built lives, families, connections to the human world—chose to stay, accepting the fading of their power in exchange for the lives they had made.

Horus appeared at the edge of the light, his divine form diminished by the greater radiance but still proud. "I will stay," he said. "This world needs gods, even diminished ones. And I will not abandon those who depend on me."

Thoth, predictably, chose to go. "The knowledge," he breathed, his instruments already packed. "The things I could learn..."

Set gathered his followers, those who had supported his coup, and led them toward the light. "We will return stronger," he promised, looking back at Ra one final time. "This isn't over, brother. It will never be over."

"I know," Ra said. "But it doesn't have to be war."

Set paused, then nodded—a gesture of acknowledgment, if not friendship. "Perhaps. Perhaps not."

The Children of Darkness dissolved, their purpose revealed as misunderstanding, their fear exposed as ignorance. Some followed Set into the light; others stayed, ashamed, seeking redemption in the new world being born.

And through it all, Aten glowed, the bridge between worlds, her small form containing infinite possibility.

"The signal requires activation," the voice said, gentle but firm. "A choice must be made. The bridge, once opened, cannot be easily closed."

"What kind of choice?" Ra asked.

"Sacrifice." The word hung in the air, heavy with meaning. "To establish the connection permanently, to allow travel between worlds, a price must be paid. Energy must be provided. Life must be... transformed."

"No." Issah's voice was fierce. "Not my daughter. Not her life."

"Not her life," the voice agreed. "But the life of one who loves her. One who is willing to give everything so that she might fulfill her purpose."

The understanding crashed over Ra like a wave. He had known, somehow, from the moment the light descended. This was the price of his choice, the cost of his love, the final sacrifice that would prove his transformation from god to mortal to something else entirely.

"I will do it," he said.

"Ra!" Issah's cry was anguished. "No!"

"Yes." He turned to face her, his wife, his love, the reason he had given up everything. "I've lived eight thousand years, Issah. I've been a god, a ruler, a symbol. But I've only truly lived since I met you. Only truly loved since I became mortal. And I would rather have these few years of real life than another eight millennia of empty power."

"But Aten needs you. I need you."

"Aten needs the bridge. The world needs the connection. And you..." He cupped her face in his hands, feeling her tears on his palms. "You need to live. To raise our daughter. To show her what love means. To build the future I won't see."

"I can't do this alone."

"You're not alone." He kissed her forehead, her cheeks, her lips. "You have Kiya, Meritamen, Horus, everyone who stood with us. You have Aten. And you have my love, which will be with you always, even when I'm gone."

The light was growing stronger, more insistent. The time for choice was running out.

"I love you," Issah whispered.

"I love you," he replied. "Forever."

He turned to his daughter, this miracle child who had changed everything, and lifted her from Issah's arms. She looked at him with eyes that held the wisdom of ages, the innocence of infancy, the love of a child for her father.

"Be good," he told her, his voice breaking. "Be brave. Be the bridge that connects worlds, that brings understanding, that proves love is stronger than fear."

She reached up and touched his face, her small hand warm against his cheek. And in that touch, he felt the connection form—the bridge establishing itself, the energy flowing, the sacrifice beginning.

"I'm ready," he said to the light.

"As you choose," the voice replied.

The transformation began.
