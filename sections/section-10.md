The pain started before dawn, waking Issah from dreams she couldn't remember. It began as a tightness in her lower back, a sensation like a fist slowly closing, and spread around her belly in waves that made her gasp for breath.

She lay still in the darkness, trying to convince herself it was normal. The healers had warned her that the pregnancy would be difficult—no one had ever carried a hybrid child before, and her body was learning a new kind of creation. Some discomfort was expected.

But when the third wave hit, stronger than the last, she knew something was wrong.

"Ra."

He woke instantly, his mortal body still carrying the alertness of a god who had once needed to respond to prayers at any hour. "What is it?"

"I need the healer. Now."

He didn't ask questions. He was out of bed and dressed in moments, his face pale in the pre-dawn gloom. "I'll fetch her. Don't move."

"Ra."

He paused at the door, looking back at her.

"Hurry."

The pain continued in waves, each one peaking higher than the last. Issah focused on her breathing, trying to remember the techniques the healers had taught her. But fear was a tight band around her chest, making each breath shallow and insufficient.

Six months. The child wasn't ready. If it came now...

She pushed the thought away, concentrating instead on the small details of their room: the texture of the linen sheets, the crack in the ceiling plaster, the way the first light of dawn was beginning to paint the walls in shades of rose and gold. This was how she had survived as a slave, focusing on the immediate, refusing to let her mind wander to futures she couldn't control.

But she wasn't a slave anymore. She was a queen, a leader, a mother. And her child was dying inside her.

The healer arrived with Ra, a Shetu woman named Sekhet who had trained in both human and divine medicine. Her face was grave as she examined Issah, her cool fingers pressing against the swell of her belly, her sharp eyes watching the patterns of pain.

"The child is distressed," Sekhet said finally, her voice carefully neutral. "Its growth has been too rapid—the body cannot sustain the pace."

"What does that mean?" Ra demanded, his hand gripping Issah's with desperate strength.

"It means the pregnancy is failing. The child is drawing too much, too fast. Another month, perhaps two, and they will both die."

The words fell like stones into still water. Issah felt them settle in her chest, heavy and cold.

"There must be something," Ra said. "Some treatment, some remedy."

"There is one option." Sekhet's eyes met Issah's, and she saw compassion there, but also resignation. "Blood. Shetu blood, in large quantities. It would sustain the child, allow it to develop at a rate the body can manage."

"Then do it," Issah said. "Take what you need."

"It's not that simple." Sekhet hesitated, looking toward Ra. "The blood must be given willingly, by those with divine lineage. And it carries risks. The child might become dependent, unable to survive without regular feeding. It might develop... traits... that make it more Shetu than human."

"Or it might simply die without it," Issah said. "Those are the choices? Blood now, or death later?"

"Essentially, yes."

Ra moved to the window, staring out at the brightening sky. His shoulders were rigid with tension, his silence stretching long enough that Issah began to fear his answer.

"Ra," she said softly.

"My blood would work," he said, not turning. "Even mortal, I carry the divine lineage. Eight thousand years of godhood don't simply vanish."

"You said you couldn't access that power anymore."

"I said I couldn't wield it. But it's still in me, in my blood, in my bones." He turned to face them, and Issah saw determination burning in his eyes. "If my blood can save our child, then take it. All of it, if necessary."

"Ra, no—"

"I was willing to give up my godhood to be with you," he said, moving to kneel beside the bed. "What is blood compared to that? What is any sacrifice too great for our child?"

Sekhet watched their exchange with eyes that had seen too much. "The process would be gradual. Small transfusions over weeks, monitoring the response. If his blood is compatible..."

"It will be," Ra said. "It must be."

The first transfusion happened that morning, in the small room that served as their home. Sekhet worked with efficient precision, drawing blood from Ra's arm and preparing it for Issah's body. The process was uncomfortable—Ra's mortal form felt every needle prick, every sensation magnified by his new vulnerability—but he never flinched.

Issah felt the blood enter her like warmth spreading from a fire. Almost immediately, the pain began to ease, the tightness in her belly relaxing. She placed her hand on her stomach and felt the child move—not with distress now, but with something like contentment.

"It's working," Sekhet said, surprise evident in her voice. "The child is responding."

"How much will it need?" Issah asked.

"More than he can safely give alone." Sekhet looked at Ra, assessing. "His mortal form has limits. Regular donations will weaken him, perhaps dangerously. We'll need additional donors."

"Who else has divine blood?"

"Horus. Set. Thoth. The other remaining gods." Sekhet's expression darkened. "But whether they would help..."

"Horus wants my child dead," Issah said. "Set... I don't know what Set wants."

"Thoth," Ra said slowly. "He might help. We've had our differences, but he values knowledge above all. The first hybrid child would be... an opportunity for study."

"An opportunity for study?" Issah's voice rose. "Our child is not an experiment!"

"I know." Ra took her hand, his fingers cold from blood loss. "But if Thoth's curiosity can save its life, then let him study. Let him measure and observe and catalog. As long as our child lives, I don't care about his motivations."

Issah looked at him—at the pallor of his skin, the tremor in his hand, the desperate love in his eyes—and felt her anger dissolve. He was right. Pride had no place here, no room in a struggle for survival.

"Send for Thoth," she said to Sekhet. "Tell him... tell him we have something that requires his particular expertise."

The healer nodded and left, her footsteps fading into the morning sounds of the city. Ra and Issah were alone, connected by the blood that now flowed between them, by the child they were fighting to save.

"Will it be enough?" Issah asked.

"I don't know." Ra climbed onto the bed beside her, careful not to disturb the bandage on his arm. "But it's more than we had yesterday. And tomorrow we'll have more still."

She leaned into him, feeling his warmth, his heartbeat, his presence. They had faced impossible odds before. They had faced death and returned from it. This was simply another battle, another test of their commitment to each other and to the future they were trying to build.

"Whatever happens," she whispered, "we face it together."

"Together," he agreed.

Outside, the sun rose fully over Memphis, painting the city in light that had once been Ra's to command. Now it belonged to Horus, to the new generation of gods who would shape the world in ways Ra never had.

But in this small room, in this moment, they had created something new. Something that belonged to neither human nor Shetu, to neither god nor mortal. Something that was simply theirs.

The child moved again, stronger now, responding to the blood that sustained it. And Issah allowed herself to hope, just a little, that they might yet win this fight.
