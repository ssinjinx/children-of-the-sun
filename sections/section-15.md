The news of Sekhet's betrayal spread through Memphis like wildfire in dry grass, consuming the fragile trust that had held the transitional government together. By noon, the council chamber was in chaos—accusations flying like arrows, factions forming and dissolving in moments, the careful balance of the past two years shattering before their eyes.

Ra watched from the gallery, Issah at his side, as the government they had built crumbled under the weight of revealed treachery. Meritamen struggled to maintain order, her voice hoarse from shouting, her authority evaporating with each new revelation.

"Khufu has fled," Kiya reported, appearing at Ra's elbow with the silence of a shadow. "Anhert was found dead in his chambers—poison, likely self-administered. The Children of Darkness are covering their tracks, sacrificing pawns to protect their leadership."

"And the leadership?"

"Still hidden. Still dangerous." Kiya's eyes scanned the chaos below. "Without Sekhet to guide them, they may strike early, before we're ready."

Ra nodded, his face grim. The trap they had set required time—time to position their forces, time to prepare the ground, time to ensure that when the Children of Darkness came for his child, they would find only defeat. But time was running out.

Below, the council had devolved into open conflict. Human delegates accused Shetu members of conspiracy; Shetu representatives demanded protection from persecution; moderates from both sides tried vainly to restore order while extremists seized the opportunity to push their agendas.

"This is what they wanted," Issah said quietly. "Not just to kill our child. To destroy everything we've built. To prove that cooperation between human and Shetu is impossible."

"Then we prove them wrong." Ra took her hand, squeezing it for strength they both needed. "We rebuild. Again. As many times as it takes."

But even as he spoke, he saw the fractures widening. A human delegate from the agricultural districts stood, his face purple with rage: "How can we trust any of them? They've been feeding on us for millennia, and now they're plotting murder in our midst!"

A Shetu elder rose to respond, his voice like ice: "We fed on you? We elevated you, protected you, guided your civilization from mud huts to marble cities. And this is your gratitude?"

"Guidance?" The human laughed, harsh and bitter. "You mean slavery. You mean treating us like cattle, like food, like—"

"Enough!" Issah's voice cut through the tumult, carrying the authority she had earned in revolution and governance. She moved to the railing, looking down at the chaos with eyes that had seen too much death to be intimidated by mere shouting. "Enough."

The chamber fell silent, shocked by her intervention.

"Sekhet betrayed us all," Issah continued, her voice steady despite the tremor in her hands. "Not just me, not just my child. She betrayed the dream we have been building together. And if we let her, if we let the Children of Darkness, turn us against each other, then she wins even from a prison cell."

"What would you have us do?" someone called from the crowd. "Pretend everything is fine? Ignore the fact that our most trusted healer was a traitor?"

"I would have you remember why we fought the revolution in the first place." Issah's voice dropped, became intimate, as if she were speaking to each person individually. "Not so humans could rule instead of Shetu. Not so Shetu could maintain their power. But so we could build something new. Something better. A world where the strong don't prey on the weak, where divine blood doesn't mean divine right, where love isn't limited by the accident of birth."

She placed her hand on her belly, the gesture visible to everyone in the chamber. "My child represents that future. Some of you fear it. Some of you hate it. But it is coming whether you welcome it or not. The only question is whether we meet it together, as one people, or divided, destroying each other while the real enemy watches and laughs."

The silence that followed was deeper than before, filled with thought rather than anger.

"We need a new council," Ra said, stepping forward to stand beside her. "One cleansed of traitors, yes, but also one expanded to include more voices, more perspectives, more accountability. The old structure failed because it trusted too much in tradition. We need something new."

"And who will lead this new council?" Meritamen asked, her voice tired but hopeful. "Who has the trust of both sides?"

"Issah." The word came from multiple voices, spreading through the chamber like ripples in a pond. "Issah should lead."

Ra felt his wife stiffen beside him. She had never sought power, had accepted leadership only from necessity. But he also felt her straighten, felt the resolve that had carried her from slavery to revolution settle into her bones.

"I will serve," she said, "if you will have me. Not as queen, not as conqueror, but as steward. A caretaker for the future we're trying to build."

The vote was not unanimous—nothing in such divided times could be—but it was sufficient. The council reformed around her, human and Shetu working together to establish new safeguards, new transparency, new bonds of trust that would hopefully prove stronger than the old.

But the fracture had happened, and the scars would remain. Three delegates resigned rather than serve under a "traitor's consort." Two Shetu elders walked out, vowing to establish their own council in the old territories. The government that emerged from the chaos was smaller, weaker, but perhaps—Ra hoped—more honest.

As the session finally ended and the delegates filed out, some reconciled, others nursing new grudges, Ra turned to Issah and saw the exhaustion in her eyes.

"You were magnificent," he said.

"I was terrified." She leaned against him, letting him support her weight. "I don't know if I can hold it together, Ra. The divisions run so deep."

"You don't have to hold it alone." He kissed her forehead, tasting the salt of her sweat. "We'll do it together. All of us. Kiya, Meritamen, Set, even Horus in his way. We're building a network, Issah. A web of people who believe in the future."

"And those who don't?"

"We convince them. Or we outlast them." He guided her toward the exit, toward the sunlight and the rest of their lives. "The future belongs to those who prepare for it, not those who fear it. We've been preparing for two years. The Children of Darkness have been preparing for decades. But we have something they don't."

"What?"

"Love." He smiled at her surprise. "Love, and hope, and the stubborn refusal to give up. Those are stronger than fear and tradition, Issah. I truly believe that."

She looked at him, this former god who had given up everything to be with her, and felt her own hope rekindle. "Then let's prove it. Together."

"Together."

They stepped out into the afternoon sun, into a city scarred by betrayal but still standing, into a future uncertain but not hopeless. The government had fractured, yes. But sometimes, Ra thought, fractures were necessary. They showed where the weaknesses were, where the work needed to be done.

And they reminded you that even broken things could be mended, if you were willing to put in the effort.
