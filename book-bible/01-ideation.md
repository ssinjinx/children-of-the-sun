### One-Sentence Hook
After Ra gives up his immortality to be with Issah, they must navigate a world where gods are falling, humans are rising, and the children of their forbidden union hold the key to humanity's future—or its destruction.

### One-Paragraph Synopsis
The revolution has come. The Shetu's divine deception has been exposed, and the gods who ruled Egypt for millennia are falling. Ra, once the Sun King, is now merely a man—mortal, vulnerable, and deeply in love with the former slave who changed everything. But their happiness is threatened by the consequences of their union: Issah carries Ra's child, a being that is neither fully human nor fully Shetu. As the old world burns and a new one struggles to be born, Ra and Issah must protect their family from those who would see their child as either a savior or a monster. Meanwhile, other Shetu are awakening to the possibility of humanity, and the last survivors of the crashed ships may finally have found a way home—if they're willing to pay the ultimate price.

### Tone & Mood
Darker and more epic than Book 1. The romance is established but tested by external pressures. Themes of parenthood, sacrifice, and what it means to be human. The scope expands from intimate (two people) to societal (a world in transition).

### Comparable Titles (Comps)
1. *A Court of Mist and Fury* by Sarah J. Maas (established couple tested)
2. *The Priory of the Orange Tree* by Samantha Shannon (epic scope, divine beings)
3. *Circe* by Madeline Miller (mythological retelling, divine/mortal relationships)

### Target Audience
Readers who loved *Slave to the Gods* and want to see:
- The romance deepen and mature
- The world expand beyond the temple
- Higher stakes with real consequences
- Exploration of parenthood and legacy
- The revolution actually happening

### Key Themes
- **Sacrifice** — What parents give up for their children
- **Identity** — What makes us human? Blood? Choice? Love?
- **Legacy** — The weight of what we leave behind
- **Revolution** — Building a new world from the ashes of the old
- **Family** — The families we're born into vs. the families we choose

### Notes
- Book 1 ended with Ra choosing Issah over godhood
- The resistance has won—or have they?
- The Shetu are not gone, just exposed
- The child is the first human-Shetu hybrid
- We need to see the other gods' reactions to Ra's choice
- Thoth's search for a way home becomes urgent

### Central Question
Can love survive when the world that made it possible is destroyed?
